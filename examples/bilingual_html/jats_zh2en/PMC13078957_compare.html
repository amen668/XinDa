随着低剂量螺旋计算机断层扫描（low-dose computed tomography, LDCT）筛查普及，磨玻璃影（ground glass opacity, GGO）或磨玻璃结节（ground glass nodule, GGN）检出率显著升高[1]，已成为胸外科、呼吸科及肿瘤科等临床常见问题[2]。GGO为宽泛影像学征象，指肺内稍高密度影，边界可清晰或模糊，可见肺血管及支气管，无大小限制，GGN是GGO的特殊类型，特指薄层CT肺窗上类圆形、直径≤30 mm的GGO。所有GGN本质上均为GGO，仅满足“类圆形、直径≤30 mm”条件的GGO才属GGN范畴。此类病变具有与传统实性肺癌不同的生物学特性：生长缓慢、转移潜能低、预后较好[3,4]。其疾病谱系包括良性病变、非典型腺瘤样增生（atypical adenomatous hyperplasia, AAH）、原位腺癌（adenocarcinoma in situ, AIS）、微浸润腺癌（minimally invasive adenocarcinoma, MIA）及浸润性腺癌（invasive adenocarcinoma, IAC），其中病理确诊为腺癌的GGO即为磨玻璃样肺腺癌[5]。上述谱系特征为个体化、精准化管理提供重要依据，也使临床在干预时机与治疗强度选择上面临新挑战[2]。本共识主要探讨影像学表现为GGO的肺部病变管理，重点关注病理确诊为肺腺癌者（即磨玻璃样肺腺癌）。为便于表述并与多数临床研究及指南术语一致，下文涉及筛查、诊断、随访及部分治疗决策时，主要使用“GGO”这一涵盖性术语，特指经病理证实为腺癌的磨玻璃病变时，使用“磨玻璃样肺腺癌”。

当前GGO诊疗领域存在诸多尚未达成共识的争议焦点，贯穿疾病管理全程：（1）诊断层面：如何依据影像学特征[如大小、实性成分占比（consolidation-to-tumor ratio, CTR）、形态变化]精准预测病变浸润度与侵袭力?（2）治疗策略争议尤为突出：持续存在的纯GGO（pure GGO, pGGO），应手术干预还是积极随访?若手术，时机如何选择[2]?术式如何权衡（亚肺叶切除与肺叶切除）[6,7]?多发病灶应根治性切除还是选择性处理[8]?随访强度、频率及持续时间如何设定[9]?这些关键问题尚无全球统一“金标准”，不同学术流派及诊疗中心实践差异显著，构成临床决策“灰色地带”。这一决策困境并非GGO领域独有，在癌症防治中具有普遍性，如携带乳腺癌易感基因（breast cancer susceptibility gene, BRCA）突变个体面临预防性乳腺切除术抉择时，同样需在“早诊早治”与“过度治疗”间权衡科学证据与个体价值取向。

面对上述争议，传统“家长式”或单纯技术导向的医患沟通模式存在明显局限[10]。医生单方面依据指南或个人经验决策，或将复杂信息过度简化让患者被动选择，均难以应对GGO诊疗的高度不确定性、策略多样性及结局差异性。患者非被动接受者，其价值观、生活目标、疾病认知、风险耐受度、生活质量期望及对随访负担的态度，均为个体化决策重要因素。忽视患者价值取向的决策，即便技术层面“正确”，也可能导致满意度下降、依从性不佳甚至决策后悔[11]。

因此，医患共同决策（shared decision-making, SDM）在GGO诊疗这一高度依赖权衡的领域极具价值。SDM是结构化协作过程，医生需阐明疾病特征，系统呈现所有可行选项，结合证据分析各选项的潜在获益、风险及长期影响，患者充分理解信息后，表达其关切点、生活优先次序及价值偏好[12]，最终双方经沟通达成既符合最佳证据又契合患者个体情况的诊疗决策。将SDM融入GGO诊疗可优化决策质量，增强患者自主参与感与掌控感，改善医患沟通信任，提升治疗依从性及随访配合度。近期Chung等[13]发表于Chest的大样本回顾性研究显示，肺癌筛查中接受SDM的患者长期筛查依从性更高，筛查后第1年依从性提升26.5%，第4年差异扩大至32.5%，提示SDM有助于促进患者长期随访。SDM为“观察还是手术”“何时手术”“何种手术”等缺乏单一标准答案的问题，提供了可操作的决策框架。

《磨玻璃样肺腺癌医患共同决策诊疗共识》由同济大学附属上海市肺科医院多学科专家制定，旨在梳理诊疗关键环节与争议场景，明确SDM核心要素与实施流程，为临床提供可操作指导。本共识拟通过推广SDM，减少不同中心及学科间实践差异，助力医患在不确定性情景下开展结构化沟通与风险获益权衡，推动诊疗模式由“以疾病为中心”向“以患者价值观为中心”转变。本共识推荐的级别见表1。

1 筛查

1.1 筛查起始与终止年龄

GGO的筛查起始与终止年龄是临床重要议题，目前各指南推荐的筛查年龄尚不统一[14]。起始年龄争议集中于是否设统一界限及如何平衡筛查获益与风险，部分指南[1,15]建议45或50岁起始，亦有共识[5,16]提出40岁以上具备特定危险因素者筛查。这提示起始年龄非绝对标准，需结合患者吸烟史、家族史、环境暴露史及遗传背景综合判断。尤其非吸烟女性等特殊人群，应重视个体化风险评估，而非机械遵循年龄阈值。医患需充分沟通具体风险，共同商讨筛查必要性与时机，确保决策符合循证依据且尊重患者意愿。

筛查终止年龄的争议焦点，在于权衡老年群体筛查获益与潜在风险[17,18]。虽有指南推荐75岁为终止筛查上限，但更多观点支持按整体健康状况、预期寿命及治疗耐受性个体化判断[9,14,17]。高龄患者惰性GGO比例较高，筛查可能导致过度诊断与心理负担，故是否继续筛查需结合共存疾病、预期生存时间及个人意愿慎重评估[18]。尤其健康状况良好、预期寿命较长的老年人，不应仅因年龄剥夺筛查机会[19]。这一复杂决策需医患深入交流，明确筛查预期获益与可能风险，共同制定合理方案[17,19]。

青少年及年轻成人一般不纳入筛查，除非存在明确遗传高风险因素，此类情况需经多学科诊疗团队（multidisciplinary team, MDT）严格评估[16]。整个筛查决策过程中，SDM起核心作用[13]。医生需向患者阐明GGO惰性特性、筛查双面性及个体风险状况，帮助理解不同选择的依据与影响[16]，患者则充分表达担忧与价值取向，双方基于信任与合作形成共识性选择。该模式尤其适用于临界年龄或风险不明确的患者，助力在证据与偏好间找到最佳平衡。

推荐意见1：建议吸烟、职业暴露、家族史等肺癌高危个体，从40岁起考虑低剂量CT筛查（2A），非吸烟女性等特殊人群，需结合二手烟、厨房油烟等暴露史个体化评估（2B）。75岁以上患者是否继续筛查，需结合健康状况、预期寿命及个人意愿综合判断，不宜仅以高龄终止（2A）。所有筛查决策均需医患充分沟通、共享信息后共同制定，确保筛查既符合患者最佳利益，也体现个人偏好与价值取向（2A）。

1.2 筛查时间间隔

GGO筛查时间间隔是临床随访关键环节，尚无全球统一标准，争议核心在于平衡“及时发现病灶进展”与“避免过度检查及辐射、心理负担”[20,21]。首次筛查后复查时机存在分歧：有主张3-6个月短期复查排除一过性炎症，尤其针对混合型GGO（mixed GGO, mGGO）[8,9]，也有支持年度复查，认为pGGO多呈惰性生长，频繁检查获益有限[20-23]。长期稳定GGO是否坚持年度随访亦有争议，部分指南建议持续年度筛查，亦有证据支持稳定2年后适当延长间隔至1-2年，但均强调不可完全停止随访[5,23,24]。随访时长需结合患者年龄、整体健康及预期寿命综合判断，多数共识建议对GGO保持长期随访意识，因部分病变可能数年后才进展[23,24]。

临床制定筛查间隔需依托GGO类型、大小及动态变化等影像学特征，同时融入患者风险背景、心理状态和预期寿命等自身因素[23,25]。例如，实性成分多、直径大或有高危因素者通常需缩短复查间隔，多年稳定的微小pGGO可酌情延长[23,25]。此外，极度焦虑患者可酌情短期复查缓解忧虑，但需警惕过度检查，高龄或合并严重疾病者应采取更保守策略。技术一致性亦不容忽视，推荐固定机构与扫描参数保障随访可比性，复查建议采用标准剂量CT确保图像清晰，避免漏诊细微变化[1]。放射科医生需确保每次随访检查在层厚、剂量、重建算法等关键参数上一致，精准、可重复测量对比GGO，为后续决策提供可靠技术支撑。

SDM在本议题中起核心作用，医生应主动向患者阐释GGO惰性特点与随访双重性，既说明及时复查对早期发现进展的价值，也坦诚频繁检查可能的辐射暴露与心理压力[3]。制定随访计划时，须结合患者具体病情、风险层次、心理承受力及个人意愿个体化商讨，共同确定初始间隔与长期策略。Chung等[13]的研究进一步证实，肺癌筛查中接受SDM的患者长期筛查依从性更高，获益可持续多年，为SDM在GGO长期随访管理中的关键作用提供有力支持。每次复查后需重新评估并动态调整随访策略，尤其对随访间隔处于灰色地带的患者（如GGO长期稳定或年龄与预期寿命矛盾），更需充分沟通达成共识，体现“以患者为中心”的诊疗原则。

推荐意见2：pGGO<5 mm者实施年度随访，稳定后可酌情延长至1-2年（2A），新发现或直径≥5 mm的pGGO建议6-12个月首次复查（2A），mGGO或实性成分≥5 mm者缩短至3-6个月复查（2A）。所有长期稳定GGO仍须坚持随访，间隔可适度延长但不建议终止（2A）。最终随访方案需基于GGO表现、患者风险、心理状态及预期寿命个体化制定，经医患充分沟通共同决策（2A）。

2 诊断

2.1 影像学诊断的局限性

GGO影像学诊断虽是临床决策的重要依据，但其内在局限性显著影响诊疗策略的制定与医患沟通。高分辨率CT作为当前主要影像手段，对病变检出和随访不可或缺，却无法替代病理诊断，这一根本局限使其定性判断存在明显模糊性[5]。同一GGO影像既可能对应早期肺腺癌，也可能是炎症、出血或纤维化等良性病变，导致首次发现时多难以明确性质，引发立即干预或短期随访的争议，也带来患者焦虑与重复辐射暴露问题[9,18]。

即便影像高度疑似恶性，对病理亚型的预判仍有较大误差，尤其难以精准区分AAH、AIS与MIA，更可能低估病变实际浸润程度。近年研究[26]表明，CT值定量分析可一定程度辅助判断GGO病理阶段，如平均CT值>-472 HU常与IAC有关，较低值多见于前驱病变。但CT值受设备、重建算法及测量方法影响，判断阈值尚未统一，临床应用需谨慎解读。术后病理升级时有发生，直接影响手术范围确定和淋巴结清扫策略，成为术中决策与术后管理的争议焦点。此外，影像学对GGO生长速度、侵袭转移潜能等生物学行为的预测能力有限，存在评估盲区。正电子发射计算机断层显像（positron emission tomography, PET）/CT在实性成分少的GGO中诊断价值有限，淋巴结评估亦易出现假阴性，导致临床分期不确定，但在怀疑多发GGO转移或评估术后可疑复发等特定场景下，仍可提供重要代谢信息辅助综合判断。

GGO随访期影像评估同样面临测量误差与主观判断的挑战，大小、密度及实性成分变化的判定缺乏绝对客观标准，不同医师或不同时点测量可能存在差异，直接影响“进展”的定义与管理调整。对于多发GGO，影像在鉴别多原发与肺内转移、确定主导病灶等方面作用有限，需依赖分子病理等进一步技术手段，增加了临床决策复杂性[27]。面对这些局限，SDM尤为关键[13]。医生应主动向患者说明影像学结果的不确定性，包括诊断推断病理阶段的概率属性及随访中可能的误判风险，让患者理解诊疗选择的依据与权衡。放射科医生在MDT评估中需承担超越单纯描述的关键责任，应基于高分辨率CT影像特征（如大小、密度、形态、动态变化），结合CT值定量分析与定性评估，提供GGO性质（如良性可能性、浸润概率）及进展风险的初步判断，为临床决策提供直接影像学依据。

制定个体化方案时，需结合临床、影像及尽可能获取的病理信息综合判断，复杂病例强烈推荐MDT模式，集思广益弥补影像学不足[28]。同时，医生也需关注影像组学与人工智能等新兴技术在辅助诊断与预测中的应用前景，虽目前仍处探索阶段，但未来有望提升影像评估的客观性与预测精度。

推荐意见3：推荐临床医师应用影像学评估GGO时，明确其在定性诊断、病理分级及生物学行为预测中的局限性，避免仅凭影像信息作出绝对判断（2A）。可结合CT值等定量指标辅助评估，但需注意其变异性与局限性（2B）。诊疗决策中，医生须与患者充分沟通影像结果的不确定性及对后续策略的影响，共同权衡不同方案的风险与获益（2A）。疑难病例建议通过MDT模式整合各类信息，提高诊断准确性与治疗合理性，所有决策应体现患者参与及个体化原则（2A）。

2.2 侵入性诊断的必要性与风险

GGO是否需行侵入性诊断（如经皮肺穿刺、支气管镜活检），临床仍存显著争议，核心在于权衡明确病理诊断的获益与操作风险及对后续治疗的影响[25]。目前对适合手术的孤立性GGO，多数专家倾向于直接手术切除而非穿刺活检，因其兼具诊断与治疗作用，可一次完成、效率高，还能规避活检可能的假阴性、取样误差及气胸、出血、极少数针道种植等风险[2,9,29-32]。尤其GGO穿刺诊断面临独特挑战，包括病变密度低、质地软，常伴肺泡塌陷或纤维增生，穿刺针获取足够代表性组织的难度大，假阴性率高于实性结节（文献报道10%-30%），且病灶边界模糊、随呼吸移动度大，精准定位要求高，气胸等并发症风险不可忽视。此外，活检后的胸膜粘连或局部影像学改变，还可能增加后续手术难度或干扰病灶评估。

尽管如此，特定临床情境下侵入性诊断仍具重要价值[33]。影像学不典型、良性疾病（如炎症、感染）无法排除时，活检可为良恶性诊断提供参考信息，高龄、合并症多、功能状态差无法耐受手术，或患者坚决拒绝手术但需病理指导非手术治疗（如放疗、消融）时，活检亦属必要[9,33]。此外，多发GGO中具有不典型或侵袭性影像特征的病灶，活检可明确病理类型以指导整体治疗策略[8]，计划新辅助治疗时获取组织行分子检测是关键前提[34]。决定穿刺前需审慎权衡，小而纯的GGO或位置深、紧邻大血管/重要脏器的病灶，穿刺获益风险比较低，需更谨慎。

这一决策过程中SDM起核心作用[13]。医生应向患者清晰说明GGO的惰性特征，阐释直接手术与先行活检的利弊：前者效率高，但有一定手术创伤及潜在过度治疗风险，后者可能避免不必要手术，却伴随操作相关并发症、较高假阴性可能（尤其pGGO）及治疗延迟风险[35]。评估需综合考量：GGO可穿刺性（大小、位置、与血管距离）、拟用穿刺技术（如CT引导下同轴活检技术可提高取材成功率、减少胸膜通过次数）、操作者经验及病理科对少量活检组织的诊断能力。技术层面推荐用薄层CT精准规划路径，选择细针或组织芯活检针，操作中密切关注患者呼吸配合，最大化提高诊断率、降低风险。还需考虑患者整体健康状况及个人风险承受能力，充分尊重其对明确诊断或尽快治疗的偏好[25]；尤其临床判断处于灰色地带、患者对不确定性容忍度低时，更需充分沟通达成个体化选择[10,13]。

推荐意见4：高度疑似恶性且适合手术的孤立性GGO，优先考虑直接手术切除，规避侵入性诊断的额外风险及诊疗延迟（2A），无法耐受手术、患者拒绝手术，或多发病灶需明确诊断指导治疗策略时，建议行侵入性诊断获取病理结果（2A）。所有决策需在全面评估病变特征、患者整体状况及意愿基础上，经医患充分沟通共同制定（2A）。

3 治疗

3.1 手术指征与时机

GGO的手术指征与时机选择是核心争议，关键在于把握“转移成功之前的阶段”（病变获得局部或远处转移能力之前的生物学演进阶段，临床中用10年无复发生存（recurrence-free survival, RFS）率达到100%来评估此阶段），在积极干预与动态观察间取得合理平衡。部分观点支持疑似恶性GGO及时手术以规避进展风险及患者焦虑，Li等[36]对纯磨玻璃样肺腺癌术后10年生存研究证实，pGGO患者术后10年RFS率达100%，即使病理为IAC亦无复发，支持将pGGO视为可手术治愈的惰性亚型。长期随访研究[3]对135个pGGO中位随访193个月，发现即使GGO稳定10年后仍有3.9%会生长，提示惰性病变仍存进展可能。但更多证据[37]表明，多数pGGO及CTR≤0.25的mGGO具有显著惰性，处于“免疫平衡”状态，转移成功率极低。Li等[38]研究显示，直径≤2 cm且CTR≤0.25的肺部亚实性GGO，观察等待组5年无事件生存率达100%，与手术组（99.0%）呈非劣效结果（P<0.001），支持该人群采取保守策略。另一项针对1003个GGO（684例患者）的前瞻性研究[39]随访10年余，进一步证实随访组与手术组10年总生存率无显著差异，无论GGO稳定或增大，两组长期生存结局均无统计学差异，稳定与增大GGO的7年RFS率均达100%，且GGO增大不影响手术成本和时长。该研究明确CT随访可作为GGO适宜管理策略，直至出现实性成分。AIS、MIA及CTR=0的IAC切除后10年RFS率接近100%，代表典型“转移成功之前”阶段，即便随访中进展再手术，患者仍可获极高治愈率，提示存在充分“治愈窗口期”进行个体化决策[36]。

国内外指南对GGO处理差异显著，反映了不同国家对GGO生物学行为的认知不同[25,40]。肺结节诊治中国专家共识建议5-10 mm的pGGO随访增大至10 mm以上考虑手术[9,41]，日本指南将阈值提至15 mm，美国国立综合癌症网络（National Comprehensive Cancer Network, NCCN）指南推荐持续存在的pGGO直径≥20 mm才考虑切除，欧洲部分指南甚至放宽至30 mm[40]。这种差异源于人种特征、医疗资源、卫生经济学考量及对pGGO侵袭性评估的不同。需重视的是，影像学pGGO中MIA及IAC占比不容忽视，上海市肺科医院273例IAC回顾性研究[42]提示，对于密度较高的pGGO，即便直径<10 mm也应高度警惕浸润可能。Hu等[43]研究指出，结合pGGO大小、最大CT值及CT形态特征（分叶、毛刺、胸膜凹陷、空泡征、血管集束征等）构建的多参数预测模型，可显著提高IAC术前诊断准确性，为手术时机选择提供更精准依据。

持续存在的pGGO应在体积增大时手术还是等待实性成分出现?支持较早手术的观点认为，部分pGGO持续增大或实性成分增多可能提示侵袭性增强[44]。研究[6,36]显示，pGGO手术组10年RFS率为100%，随CTR升高复发风险显著增加。CTR>0.50且肿瘤>1 cm患者术后复发率达16.22%，且复发多集中于术后5年内，尤其周边型、可行亚肺叶切除的病灶，早期干预可能以较小创伤获得根本治愈。反之，主张谨慎随访的观点强调，实性成分出现是侵袭性增强的更可靠标志，过早手术可能增加过度治疗风险。需注意，气腔播散（spread through air spaces, STAS）风险随CTR升高而增加，CTR>0.50时发生率显著上升[44]，即便pGGO中也可观察到2%-11%的STAS，提示其存在一定生物学异质性，不能简单等同于“零进展风险”。因此，周边GGO且影像学高度怀疑浸润者可更积极干预；若为中央型且需肺叶切除，可严密随访至出现实性成分（CTR≤0.25），但应在淋巴结转移前完成干预。JCOG0201研究[45]对86例0.25<CTR≤0.50患者的分析显示其淋巴结转移风险增加，支持对CTR>0.25的mGGO采取更积极策略。欧洲心胸外科协会（European Association for Cardio-thoracic Surgery, EACTS）/欧洲胸外科医师学会（European Society of Thoracic Surgeons, ESTS）指南[40]强调应基于MDT评估，结合GGO动态变化、实性成分及患者风险因素综合决策。

影响手术决策的因素多样：GGO影像特征至关重要（直径、CTR及动态变化）[25]，通常直径较大（如>8 mm）、CTR>0.25或随访进展者更倾向手术[25,44]，上述IA期浸润性非小细胞肺癌（non-small cell lung cancer, NSCLC）研究[44]证实CTR>0.50是复发的重要预测因子，深分叶征、癌胚抗原（carcinoembryonic antigen, CEA）升高等也是预后不良的独立预测因子，也应充分评估[46]。患者年龄、预期寿命及心理状态同样关键，年轻患者可择期干预，高龄、合并症者宜随访，焦虑严重影响生活质量者，充分知情后可手术[25]。病理阶段预估直接影响策略，2018年《上海市肺科医院磨玻璃结节早期肺腺癌的诊疗共识（第一版）》提出，高度怀疑AIS且直径>8 mm的pGGO，若患者心理压力大可考虑手术，MIA和IAC则建议手术（MIA术后近100%治愈，IAC即使以GGO为主仍存小概率高危亚型或复发风险）[4,26]；多发pGGO常遵循“处理主病灶，随访次病灶”原则[8,25,41]。

这一复杂决策过程中SDM居核心地位。医生需清晰解释GGO的惰性特征、“转移成功之前阶段”及“治愈窗口期”等概念，结合证据说明病变通常进展缓慢，留有充分权衡时间[3,36]，客观阐述手术的潜在获益与风险、随访的意义与负担，在共同决策中充分讨论不同诊疗方案的风险获益[25]。最终方案应立足患者个体情况（GGO行为、年龄、健康状况、心理承受能力、个人价值观），通过充分沟通达成共识[25,40]。对于临界患者（如CTR约0.25），医患深入交流与共同选择尤为重要[44]。决策应保留适度弹性，尊重患者充分知情后的不同选择。当面临“手术”与“随访”取舍时，本质是在“早诊早治”与“避免过度治疗”间权衡，在根治可能性与长期管理策略间作出个体化选择[36,40]。

推荐意见5：（1）持续存在的直径较大（通常>8 mm）、实性成分较高（CTR>0.25）或随访中进展（体积增大/实性成分增多）的mGGO，高度怀疑MIA或IAC者，建议活检或手术明确诊断（1B）。（2）10 mm及以上的pGGO，随访显示进展（体积增大/密度增加），或出现空泡（囊腔）、分叶、毛刺、支气管充气征、胸膜凹陷、血管聚集等恶性征象，高度怀疑MIA或IAC，且位于肺周边或单个肺段、可行亚肺叶切除者，倾向活检或手术明确诊断；若病变位于肺中央需行肺叶切除，可酌情继续随访至实性成分出现（CTR≤0.25）。持续存在（3-6个月）的15 mm以上pGGO，倾向建议活检或手术明确诊断（1B）。（3）10 mm以下的pGGO和8 mm以下的mGGO：①高度怀疑AIS者优先随访观察，若出现明显变化（直径增大>2 mm、密度显著升高、实性成分增加），则考虑缩短复查间隔或活检、手术明确诊断；②高度怀疑MIA或IAC者应行MDT讨论，医患共同协商个体化方案，若患者心理压力极大，可适当放宽活检或手术指征（2A）。（4）所有决策需结合患者年龄、预期寿命、健康状况、心理需求、病变预估病理阶段、病变位置等多方面因素个体化制定，在SDM框架下经充分沟通完成，尊重患者意愿并保留弹性空间。在保证肿瘤学安全的前提下，优先选择创伤小、肺功能保留多的治疗策略，避免过度治疗（2A）。

3.2 手术方式选择

3.2.1 切除范围争议：亚肺叶切除与肺叶切除及淋巴结处理的个体化策略 GGO手术治疗策略是胸外科核心议题，关键在于将切除范围与淋巴结处理作为整体决策，而非独立环节[40]。现代GGO诊疗理念强调：基于肿瘤生物学特征，个体化同步确定切除范围与淋巴结处理策略，在肿瘤根治、肺功能保留及免疫功能维护间寻求最佳平衡[9,37]。

肺叶切除联合系统性淋巴结清扫作为传统标准术式，适用于实性成分多、浸润风险高或疑似淋巴结转移的病变[25]。但对于GGO为主型肺腺癌，其独特生物学行为促使我们重新审视该标准。研究证实，pGGO或CTR≤0.25的mGGO淋巴结转移率极低且预后良好。JCOG0804研究[47]显示，直径≤2 cm且CTR≤0.25病变亚肺叶切除后5年RFS率达99.7%。JCOG0802/WJOG4607L研究[6]表明，直径≤2 cm且CTR>0.50的周围型肺癌，解剖性肺段切除总生存不劣于肺叶切除。北美CALGB140503试验[7]进一步证实，直径≤2 cm周围型肺癌亚肺叶切除与肺叶切除总生存无显著差异。这些研究为亚肺叶切除应用提供坚实依据[31]。

但真实世界研究呈现更复杂的临床图景：美国胸外科医师协会数据库大规模研究纳入32,340例临床IA期（≤2 cm）NSCLC患者，发现肺叶切除、肺段切除的总生存及肺癌特异性生存均显著优于楔形切除，差异或与楔形切除淋巴结评估不足有关（中位取样仅3枚，Nx率达20.8%）[48]。首尔峨山医疗中心回顾1205例临床IA期肺腺癌患者发现，保证充足切缘（cT1a≥1 cm或≥肿瘤直径，cT1b≥2 cm或≥肿瘤直径）且充分淋巴结评估（≥3个N2站+≥1个N1站）前提下，楔形切除与肺段切除患者的RFS率无显著差异（5年RFS率：92.4% vs 93.3%）[49]。这些发现强调，无论何种切除方式，充足切缘与系统性淋巴结评估至关重要。

淋巴结处理策略需与切除范围同步个体化制定。Zhang等[37]III期随机对照研究（ECTOP-1009, NCT04527419）中期分析显示，CTR≤0.50的GGO为主型IAC患者中，系统性纵隔淋巴结清扫未发现转移，且清扫组手术时间、出血量、术后住院时间均显著增加，还出现乳糜胸、术中大出血等严重并发症，提示此类患者常规系统性清扫难获生存获益，反而增加创伤与并发症风险。纳入400例手术切除pGGO的多中心回顾性研究（GORDON研究）[50]通过受试者工作特征（receiver operating characteristic, ROC）曲线分析，明确pGGO侵袭性放射学预测指标：直径≥18 mm且CT值>-390 HU联合预测准确率达81.2%，曲线下面积（area under the curve, AUC）为0.874，可为术前风险分层及手术方案优化提供参考；而该研究核心结果显示，所有纳入的pGGO患者（含346例IAC）均未发现淋巴结转移，5年淋巴结RFS率达100%，提示pGGO无论是否具备高侵袭性放射学特征，均无需常规行系统性淋巴结清扫。此外，淋巴结是T细胞活化与免疫调控关键场所，2023年Cell报道的研究[51]提示免疫治疗相关应答T细胞主要源于淋巴结，过度清扫可能削弱免疫应答，因此无转移证据时，应在肿瘤学安全前提下尽量避免不必要清扫，兼顾转移控制与免疫功能保留。

基于上述证据，临床决策遵循以下原则：直径≤2 cm、CTR≤0.25的GGO，行亚肺叶切除（楔形或肺段）且不做系统性淋巴结清扫或仅采样是合理的[37]，中央型需在三维重建辅助下保证足够阴性切缘（通常≥2 cm或≥肿瘤直径）[40]。直径≤2 cm且CTR>0.50的病变，解剖性肺段切除推荐为标准选项之一，但需注意10%-20%可能存在高危病理亚型（如微乳头/实体型成分>5%）或STAS，需考虑扩大切除及淋巴结清扫范围[6]。术中冰冻病理诊断准确性是联合决策关键：提示AAH、AIS或MIA，可行亚肺叶切除且不清扫或仅采样淋巴结；贴壁样为主型IAC且影像学CTR≤0.50，可行亚肺叶切除联合叶特异性淋巴结清扫或采样；含高危亚型IAC或CTR>0.50，应行肺叶切除联合系统性淋巴结清扫。多发GGO需根据主病灶病理结果决定切除范围与淋巴结策略。

这一综合决策中SDM至关重要。医生需向患者详细说明不同术式的核心利弊：肺叶切除根治性强但创伤大、对肺功能影响显著；亚肺叶切除可保留更多肺组织，利于后续生活质量及应对多发病变，但可能伴随略高的局部复发风险[6,7]。同时结合患者意愿、生活质量期望及复发风险承受能力个体化讨论。所有决策须结合术前高分辨率CT评估、术中冰冻病理结果及患者肺功能、年龄、合并症，经医患充分沟通制定。建议在具备三维重建、胸腔镜/机器人手术平台及术中病理快速诊断的医疗中心开展个体化手术，加强MDT协作以降低决策不确定性。

推荐意见6：直径≤2 cm的GGO，CTR≤0.25可行楔形或肺段切除，0.25<CTR≤0.50行肺段切除，淋巴结行选择性清扫或仅采样（1B）。直径≤2 cm且CTR>0.50的病变，推荐解剖性肺段切除或肺叶切除，依据术中冰冻病理决定淋巴结处理范围：高危病理亚型或CTR>0.75，建议肺叶切除+系统性淋巴结清扫；贴壁样为主型且CTR为0.50-0.75，可行肺段切除+叶特异性淋巴结清扫。直径≤2 cm、CTR>0.50且肿瘤位于胸膜正下方（边缘距离<1 cm）的病变，技术上可保证足够切缘时，肺功能储备不足或基础疾病多的患者，楔形切除可作为备选项，但需至少3站纵隔淋巴结采样（1B）。病灶较大（>2 cm）、实性成分多（CTR>0.50）、位置深或疑似淋巴结转移者，优先考虑肺叶切除+系统性淋巴结清扫（1A）。所有手术方案须依据术前CT评估、术中冰冻病理及患者个体情况，经医患充分沟通共同决策（2A）。

3.2.2 微创术式优化：技术选择与成本效益 GGO微创手术目前以胸腔镜技术为核心，多种术式并存，主要包括单孔胸腔镜手术（uniportal video-assisted thoracoscopic surgery, U-VATS）、机器人辅助胸腔镜手术（robot-assisted thoracic surgery, RATS）及多孔胸腔镜手术（multiportal video-assisted thoracoscopic surgery, M-VATS），各有技术特点与适用场景[52,53]。U-VATS技术已经成熟，单一切口创伤小、美观性好，适用于绝大多数GGO手术，尤其适合周围型病变楔形切除或简单肺段切除；复杂肺段切除等精细操作，若术者经验丰富，亦可考虑应用U-VATS，但操作难度高、存在器械干扰，对术者经验要求较高[54,55]。RATS依托三维高清视野、器械灵活运动及震颤过滤等优势，在复杂肺段切除、淋巴结清扫等精细操作中表现突出，缺点是设备与手术成本高，且缺乏触觉反馈[52,56]。M-VATS应用最广泛、技术最成熟，操作稳定且适应性强，可覆盖绝大多数胸腔镜手术，但多切口设计在术后疼痛与美观上略逊于单孔[55,57]。

确定具体手术方式需综合多方面因素：病变特点与手术复杂度是关键，简单切除可根据术者熟练度及患者意愿选择U-VATS或M-VATS，复杂解剖性切除更适合RATS或M-VATS[58]。外科医生对不同术式的掌握程度也起决定作用，U-VATS学习曲线陡峭，RATS需系统培训，M-VATS技术普及度最高[53]。此外，还需考量患者诉求，如术后疼痛控制、恢复速度、美观要求及经济承受能力，年轻或费用敏感患者可能倾向U-VATS或M-VATS，部分患者为追求精准性可接受RATS[56]。医院设备条件同样制约选择，RATS依赖高端机器人系统，并非所有单位均可开展。

这一决策过程中SDM居核心地位。医生应客观全面向患者介绍各术式优缺点，包括创伤大小、疼痛程度、恢复周期、费用差异及特定病情下的适用性，并结合自身经验给出专业建议，同时需充分尊重患者意愿与价值取向，如对微创程度的期望、经济成本考量及对手术安全性与效果的权衡。最终目标是结合患者病变特征、身体条件、心理需求及医疗资源，共同确定最合适的手术方案，在保障肿瘤根治的前提下，最大限度减少创伤、促进康复并提升治疗体验[10,54]。

推荐意见7：根据GGO病变位置、大小、复杂程度及患者个体情况综合选择微创手术方式：U-VATS技术已趋成熟，适用于绝大多数GGO手术；周围型小型病变或简单术式优先考虑U-VATS；复杂解剖性切除推荐RATS或M-VATS，术者经验丰富时亦可选用U-VATS；最终决策需结合医院设备条件、外科医师技术特长及患者对创伤、康复、费用等的意愿，经医患充分沟通共同制定（2A）。所有手术均应在保证肿瘤学根治的前提下，追求最大程度微创与快速康复。

3.3 多发GGO的处理策略

多发GGO处理是胸外科临床难点与热点，核心在于准确鉴别多原发肺癌与肺内转移，在此基础上制定以根治为目标、最大化保留肺功能的个体化治疗方案[8,25,27]。Kim等[59]多中心队列研究显示，409例手术切除的亚实性肺腺癌患者中，共检出1791个同步亚实性GGO，平均随访80.6个月，仅16.9%出现生长，7.9%最终诊断为肺癌，且均为0-I期腺癌，未显著影响患者总体生存。该研究进一步指出，GGO进展主要与自身影像学特征有关（如部分实性类型、直径较大、气泡透明征或胸膜牵拉等），数量并非关键因素。这与《多发磨玻璃结节样肺癌多学科诊疗中国专家共识（2024年版）》[8]观点一致，认为多发GGO样肺癌是多原发肺癌特殊亚型，各病灶为“独立个体”而非转移灶，预后良好，总体预后取决于主病灶[41]。

临床主要依据高分辨率CT影像特征（形态、密度、分布、动态变化等）初步判别，如GGO位于不同肺叶、形态各异、生长速度不一致，多倾向多原发，但最终需结合分子病理确诊。尽管基因测序可提供重要依据，但因成本及肿瘤异质性等问题，临床推广受限。术后或活检病灶间无相同驱动基因突变，是多发GGO样肺癌明确诊断标准之一。

需注意的是，我国多发GGO检出率已达20%-40.5%，且呈年轻化趋势，70岁以上人群检出率为35%-37.52%。除影像评估外，可结合实验室检查，包括线粒体相关酶[超氧化物歧化酶（superoxide dismutase, SOD）、天门冬氨酸氨基转移酶（aspartate aminotransferase, AST）]、细胞因子[白细胞介素-6（interleukin-6, IL-6）、肿瘤坏死因子-α（tumor necrosis factor-α, TNF-α）]、免疫指标[CD4/CD8、自然杀伤（natural killer, NK）细胞]、肿瘤标志物[CEA、细胞角蛋白19片段（cytokeratin 19 fragment, CYFRA21-1）]等综合判断，提高鉴别准确性。

治疗策略已从“根治性切除”转向“保全肺功能前提下有效控制疾病”[25]。Kim等[59]研究亦支持此策略，指出同步GGO生长不显著影响生存，因此主病灶通常建议积极处理，根据大小、CTR及位置选择肺段或楔形切除以保留健康肺组织，仅主病灶符合肺叶切除指征时考虑肺叶切除。pGGO手术指征可适当从严，如干预阈值放宽至15 mm[41]。次病灶以定期随访为主，明确进展后再考虑干预。同期或分期手术需综合考量病灶分布、患者肺功能及耐受程度，双侧病灶尤其肺功能储备较差者，更适宜分次手术，避免术后肺功能严重受损，肺总切除范围不超过10个肺段[41]。这与《多发磨玻璃结节样肺癌多学科诊疗中国专家共识（2024年版）》[8]手术原则一致：优先处理主病灶、兼顾次病灶，在符合肿瘤学原则及保留肺功能前提下尽可能同期切除所有病灶，但多数情况下无需切除全部GGO，主病灶以肺叶或肺段切除为主，次病灶行肺段或楔形切除，同侧次病灶位于肺外2/3可同期亚肺叶切除，位于肺内1/3或对侧、实性成分<5 mm或CTR<0.25的病灶可密切随访或热消融[41]。淋巴结处理方面，GGO为主型病灶通常仅对主病灶采样或叶特异性清扫，不推荐常规系统性清扫[37]。术前推荐MDT讨论，整合多学科专家意见，结合人工智能辅助评估及患者意愿制定方案[8]。

非手术治疗在多发GGO管理中地位上升，尤其消融技术具一定优势。立体定向体部放疗（stereotactic body radiation therapy, SBRT）、消融等技术为无法耐受手术、次病灶进展或拒绝再次手术者提供可行选择。《多发磨玻璃结节样肺癌多学科诊疗中国专家共识（2024年版）》[8]提出，影像引导下热消融（image-guided thermal ablation, IGTA）是多发GGO样肺癌首选治疗方式之一，或者是手术切除的重要补充，手术联合IGTA（杂交手术）是新术式[41,60]。但其用于可手术患者次病灶处理仍存争议，需严格掌握适应证，结合病灶大小、位置、病理类型等综合判断[41]。此外，循环肿瘤DNA（circulating tumor DNA, ctDNA）、外泌体miRNA等无创评估技术，未来有望在风险分层与疗效监测中发挥重要作用。

整个诊疗过程中SDM居核心地位。医生需帮助患者理解，如Kim等[59]研究指出，多发GGO通常进展缓慢，可视为需长期管理的“慢性病”，治疗目标为控制进展、消除主要威胁、维持生活质量，而非追求影像学完全清除。应结合患者年龄、肺功能、心理状态及个人意愿制定方案，明确动态随访的重要性[21]。即使术后，仍需对残留或新发病灶终身监测，随访策略可参考肺部影像报告和数据系统（Lung Imaging Reporting and Data System, Lung-RADS）分类及临床肿瘤概率模型实施分层管理[1,3]。

推荐意见8：多发GGO应在MDT讨论基础上，结合病灶影像学特征、动态变化、病理类型及患者个体情况制定个体化策略（2A），MDT团队需包含胸外科、呼吸科、肿瘤科、放疗科、影像科及病理科医生。主病灶建议行亚肺叶切除（根据大小、CTR及位置选肺段或楔形切除），必要时行肺叶切除（如病灶过大，CTR过高，亚肺叶切缘不足，冰冻病理提示高危亚型、STAS等不利因素等），次病灶以定期随访为主（2A），同期手术需严格评估肺功能及手术创伤，避免同期双侧肺叶切除（2A）。淋巴结处理以主病灶影像特征及术中冰冻病理为依据，无需常规系统性清扫（2B）。不宜手术的病灶可考虑SBRT或消融等非手术治疗，其中消融技术处理多发GGO具一定优势（2B）。所有决策需经医患充分沟通共同制定，突出长期管理、肺功能保全及生活质量维护的核心目标（2A）。

3.4 非手术替代疗法的地位

GGO诊疗中SBRT、消融等非手术替代疗法有明确特定地位[15]。SBRT通过多角度高精度聚焦照射，单次给予消融性高剂量（通常4-8次完成疗程）[61]，借助剂量快速跌落技术保护正常组织，具有无创、无出血或气胸风险特点，局部控制率较高（不可手术患者5年局部控制率达93%）[62]，但需注意远期诱发二次肿瘤风险，尤其青少年患者需慎重[63]。消融属微创操作，主要包括射频消融（radiofrequency ablation, RFA）、微波消融（microwave ablation, MWA）、冷冻消融（cryoablation, CA），经皮穿刺产生热或冷效应直接毁损肿瘤，能较好保留肺功能且可重复实施，尤其处理多发GGO具有一定优势，但存在气胸（发生率10%-60%）、出血等穿刺相关风险[60,64]。两者均无法有效获取病理标本用于诊断及分子分析，此为固有局限[65]。

SBRT已成为不可耐受手术（如心肺功能差、高龄、合并症多）或拒绝手术的早期周围型NSCLC患者的标准替代根治方案[15]。应用需严格把握适应证（通常为T1-2N0M0，直径≤5 cm），中央型病灶（近端支气管树2 cm内）需特别谨慎，采用更保守分割方案（如5-9 Gy/次）以降低支气管瘘、大出血等严重风险。消融技术各有特点：MWA能量高、升温快，更能克服“热沉效应”，适于较大病灶或近血管者；CA镇痛效果好且或可激发免疫应答，适于胸膜旁病灶；RFA应用最经典。消融成功关键在于保证消融边界（消融后需超出GGO肿瘤边缘至少5-10 mm）[60,66]。

但对于多发GGO，两种技术在处理对侧或同侧次病灶、避免多次手术创伤方面具独特价值。但身体条件适宜手术的患者，目前缺乏高级别证据[如大规模III期随机对照试验（randomized controlled trial, RCT）]证明非手术疗法与手术长期生存等效，因此手术仍是首选金标准[15]。尽管热消融在部分场景（如肺叶内、明确进展但患者无法/不愿立即肺叶切除的病灶）可作为延缓手术、保留肺功能的过渡或替代选择，但其临床应用仍存争议，国内外指南均严格限制其作为首选方案[60]。ROSEL/STARS等研究[67]虽显示SBRT有积极结果，但证据级别有限，两者在此群体中应用应视为探索性选择。选择SBRT或消融需经MDT综合评估：中央型或较大病灶更倾向SBRT，周围型或小病灶（尤其≤3 cm）可考虑消融，尤适用于希望一次完成治疗、费用敏感或需处理多个分散病灶的患者，需充分考虑本地技术条件与经验[68]。

肺叶内中央型进展病灶的临床决策尤为困难[59]。传统策略包括继续随访至符合肺叶切除指征再手术，或对进展灶先行消融，复发后再考虑手术。后者虽可能为部分患者争取肺功能保留机会，但缺乏远期疗效数据支持，须谨慎采用[60]。此时更应强调SDM，结合病灶特征、进展速度、患者肺功能基线及个人意愿，全面权衡消融、手术及主动监测的利弊。

整个决策过程中SDM居核心地位。医生需全面客观地向患者介绍手术、SBRT、消融及主动监测等策略的利弊，包括手术的根治优势与创伤风险、非手术疗法的微创特点与不确定性（如无法有效获取病理、长期数据有限、SBRT对可手术者的争议性、消融的穿刺风险及疗效评估特殊性等），帮助患者依据自身病情、价值观及生活质量要求做合理选择[62]。尤其临界状态或存在多种可能性的患者，更需充分沟通明确治疗目标与预期，共同制定个体化方案。治疗后须长期规律随访，采用增强CT（消融后需注意“煎蛋征”“蛋壳征”等特有影像演变规律）或PET/CT（建议SBRT后6个月进行）等手段，及时评估疗效并监测复发或新发病变[60]。

推荐意见9：SBRT和消融作为GGO非手术替代疗法，主要适用于不可耐受手术或拒绝手术的早期肺癌患者，及多发GGO次要病灶（主要病灶首选手术）的处理（2A）。SBRT是不可手术早期肺癌的标准选择，具体剂量和分割方案需根据结节大小和位置进行选择（2A）。消融技术选择（RFA、MWA、CA）需根据病灶特点（大小、位置、与血管关系）、设备可用性及操作者经验个体化决定，确保足够消融边界（2B）。肺叶内中央型进展、需保留肺功能或暂不宜手术者，经MDT评估及医患充分共同决策后，可审慎消融作为过渡处理（2A）。若非手术方案纳入考量，须经MDT评估并充分告知患者各自风险获益及不确定性后，审慎选择SBRT或消融作为过渡处理（2A）。所有决策需医患共同参与制定，确保患者充分知情并兼顾疗效与生活质量，治疗后须长期规律随访（2A）。

3.5 术中冰冻病理（frozen section, FS）的可靠性

FS是GGO手术决策的关键，也是外科与病理科的重要会诊环节[69]。其价值在于30 min内快速评估切除标本，为术者明确病变性质、指导切除范围及淋巴结处理方案提供即时依据[70]。但诊断可靠性存在固有局限，主要体现为术中冰冻与最终石蜡病理的不符率，国内外报道总体一致率超95%，而GGO的“诊断升级”（冰冻报MIA或AIS，石蜡病理确诊IAC）尤为值得关注，发生率为5%-15%[71]。

这种差异源于三方面：一是取材误差，病理医生需数分钟内肉眼选取标本“最重”病变区域，小而形态不规则的GGO精准定位浸润灶难度大；二是制片技术局限，冰冻切片易受冰晶伪影、组织皱褶（肺组织柔软更易发生）等影响，切片质量（甲、乙、丙三级）直接关联诊断清晰度；三是诊断判读难度，时间压力下区分AAH、AIS、MIA及贴壁型IAC等形态学差异细微病变，存在主观性和“灰区”[69]。需注意，研究[72]显示<2 cm、CTR<0.50的GGO，即使约6.3%出现术中低估（升级为IAC），患者淋巴结转移率仍极低，5年RFS率达100%，提示此类诊断升级通常不影响良好预后[72]。

这种不确定性引发临床决策争议：一方主张完全信任FS结果，对影像学符合GGO优势型、直径≤2 cm且FS报AIS/MIA的病灶，仅行亚肺叶切除且不清扫淋巴结，依据是术后即使升级，亚肺叶切除远期疗效足够，可通过密切随访管理不确定性；另一方更谨慎，建议即使FS结果良好，仍考虑解剖性肺段切除（保证切缘）或淋巴结采样[6,7]。当前共识更倾向前者，尤其在JCOG0804等高级别证据支持下，特定条件GGO病变采取限制性切除及有限淋巴结处理已获广泛认可[31]。

术后石蜡病理升级是否需二次手术补做淋巴结清扫仍存争议[40]。主流观点[37]认为，初次未行淋巴结清扫、最终病理为贴壁样为主型IAC且影像学无淋巴结转移证据者，首选密切随访而非预防性二次手术，因二次手术创伤可能大于潜在获益。

提升FS可靠性可采取多项策略：术中对可疑区域充分取材、加强外科与病理医生实时沟通（尤其结合术前CT影像特征如CTR综合判断）、联合术中细胞学印片等技术互补。诊疗全程中SDM居核心地位。医生术前需向患者详细说明FS的作用与局限性，明确告知诊断升级可能性及通常良好的预后意义，共同商讨不同情景的应对策略。患者需理解随访重要性，参与制定个体化手术方案，在微创与肿瘤学安全性间达成平衡。

推荐意见10：FS病理应作为GGO手术决策的重要参考，但非唯一依据（2A）。FS提示AIS或MIA，行亚肺叶切除及免淋巴结清扫为合理标准方案（1B）；术后石蜡病理升级为贴壁为主型IAC，无淋巴结转移临床或影像学证据者，首选密切随访而非二次手术（2B）。所有决策需结合术前高分辨率CT特征、术中所见及病理结果综合判断，术前与患者充分沟通冰冻诊断的潜在不确定性及各类预后情景，共同制定手术及术后随访计划（2A）。

4 术后康复

4.1 快速康复流程（enhanced recovery after surgery, ERAS）的个体化制定

ERAS理念在GGO围术期的个体化应用，是提升手术安全性与康复质量的重要途径[73]。核心是结合GGO病理惰性强、淋巴结转移率低、多采用亚肺叶切除保肺术式的特点，针对性调整ERAS流程，在保障根治效果的同时，最大程度减少创伤、缓解应激、加速恢复[74]。鉴于GGO患者早期肺癌占比高、高龄合并症者增多且微创手术（VATS/RATS）为主流，全面实施ERAS可实现住院时间缩短30%-50%、并发症减少的目标[53]。

个体化ERAS方案需综合多因素制定[74]。病变特征层面，小型周边型GGO、FS提示AIS/MIA者，可推行激进微创策略，优先U-VATS，力争术中“无管化”（不留胸管或用细管）或早期拔管<450 mL/24 h即可），避免常规放导尿管，最大化器官微创与快速康复；需肺叶切除或淋巴结清扫的浸润性癌，则在彻底切除基础上优化镇痛与恢复措施。患者因素同样关键，需全面进行术前心肺功能评估[如预计术后第一秒用力呼气容积（predicted postoperative forced expiratory volume in one second, ppoFEV1）/预计术后一氧化碳弥散量（predicted postoperative diffusing capacity of the lung for carbon monoxide, ppoDLCO）、心肺运动试验测最大摄氧量（maximal oxygen uptake, VO2max）]，年轻体能好者可适用日间手术及激进康复计划，高龄、合并症多或高危（吸烟史≥400年支、临界肺功能）者，需强调术前预康复（营养支持、戒烟≥4周、体育康复训练），谨慎调整ERAS措施，优先保障安全与耐受[75]。近期前瞻性随机对照研究[76]支持按患者风险分层实施多梯度个体化ERAS，依手术复杂度、患者基线状况与合并症划分康复强度等级，在安全前提下最大化康复效益。

麻醉与术中管理直接影响ERAS效果。推荐肺保护性通气，联合区域麻醉（如椎旁阻滞）与全身麻醉的多模式镇痛[常规用对乙酰氨基酚和非甾体抗炎药（nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs, NSAIDs）]，减少阿片类依赖[77]；实施目标导向液体管理（首选平衡晶体液，避免过量输注）[78]；采取主动保温及多模式措施预防术后恶心呕吐[79]。这些措施可减少手术应激，为早期下床、进食奠定基础。

术后管理聚焦加速康复核心环节[80]：（1）疼痛管理需精准，依托区域镇痛减少阿片类对胃肠功能的影响，联合物理疗法促恢复；（2）鼓励术后24 h内下床，尽早恢复经口进食（术前2 h可进清液，术后当天进食）；（3）强化肺康复训练（如激励式肺量计吸气训练、功率自行车或爬楼梯训练），改善呼吸功能与运动耐力[80,81]。GGO患者预后卓越（如AIS/MIA 5年RFS率近100%），术后随访可适当简化（如术后2年内每年1次），无需常规行头颅磁共振成像（magnetic resonance imaging, MRI）、骨扫描或PET/CT，可借助远程医疗管理，契合“手术在院、康复在家”的快速康复路径[82]。

围术期全程SDM是个体化ERAS的关键。医生需充分沟通患者病变特点、拟行术式及适配的康复路径，帮助理解GGO惰性、“转移成功之前阶段”与“治愈窗口期”概念，合理设定康复期望；尊重患者对疼痛控制、恢复速度及生活质量的诉求，共商术前预康复、术中麻醉与术式选择、术后镇痛及早期活动等具体措施。ERAS成功实施需MDT协作，胸外科、麻醉科、营养科、康复科及护理团队共同制定执行全流程优化方案，通过持续沟通与动态评估，优化康复路径，提升治疗依从性与整体满意度。

推荐意见11：推荐将ERAS理念全面融入GGO围术期管理，依据病变特征（分期、CTR）、手术方式（亚肺叶/肺叶切除）及患者情况（年龄、肺功能、合并症）实施个体化优化。早期GGO强烈推荐微创术式、精准淋巴结处理、激进管道管理（早期拔管/无管化）及强化术后肺康复；复杂手术或高危患者需在保障安全前提下调整ERAS措施，强调术前预康复。所有ERAS方案须经SDM制定，在MDT协作下执行，确保患者充分理解并参与康复，实现减少创伤、降低并发症、缩短住院时间及改善治疗体验的目标（2A）。

4.2 呼吸康复的必要性与强度分层

呼吸康复在GGO患者围术期管理中具有重要地位，是ERAS理念的关键组成[83]。核心价值在于通过系统功能训练与健康指导，有效降低术后肺部并发症风险，改善肺功能、运动耐量及生活质量，缓解疾病与手术相关心理压力[84]。因患者年龄、基础肺功能、运动能力、营养状态及手术范围差异较大，呼吸康复方案需强调个体化，依据术前评估科学分层设定强度，实现安全与有效统一。

根据术前评估指标（年龄、肺功能损害程度、运动耐量、营养状况、吸烟史、呼吸肌力量等），可划分不同康复强度层次：高龄、肺功能中重度受损、运动耐力低或营养不良者，术前建议2-4周中高强度康复训练以提升手术耐受性；肺功能基本正常或轻度受损、体能较好者，1周左右预康复即可[75,85,86]。运动形式应涵盖有氧训练、抗阻训练、呼吸肌专项训练，结合气道廓清技术、早期下床活动及健康教育与心理支持，形成多维度干预体系[83,85,86]。全程需密切监测患者反应，避免过度疲劳，识别发热、血小板严重降低或肿瘤骨转移等禁忌证，保障康复安全。

SDM对呼吸康复的制定与实施至关重要[87,88]。医生需向患者详细说明呼吸康复的意义、预期获益及潜在风险，帮助其认识到对术后恢复、减少并发症的积极作用[85]，同时充分了解患者体力状况、个人意愿及实施条件，共商可行的康复目标与计划。例如，年轻患者若期望尽快恢复日常活动，可适当提升运动强度；高龄、合并多种疾病且耐受性差者，应以安全为首要原则，采用循序渐进方案[89]。通过持续沟通与动态调整，建立医患信任合作关系，提升康复依从性与整体效果。

推荐意见12：建议所有手术GGO患者接受个体化、分层次呼吸康复干预。术前依据年龄、肺功能、运动耐量、营养状态等评估康复需求，给予1-4周中高强度预康复训练，术后尽早下床，结合有氧、抗阻训练及呼吸技巧训练促进功能恢复。康复方案须经医患共同讨论制定，兼顾科学性、安全性与可执行性，实施中保持沟通调整，最大程度改善围术期体验与远期生活质量（2A）。

4.3 术后疼痛管理策略

GGO术后疼痛管理是ERAS路径关键环节，策略已从药物依赖或单一手段镇痛，转变为以超声引导区域阻滞为基础、联合多类镇痛药的个体化多模式镇痛[53,77]。有效疼痛控制既是舒适化医疗需求，也是促进术后心肺脑等系统功能恢复、加速康复的关键，完善的疼痛管理可显著降低术后肺不张、肺部感染发生率及死亡率。核心方案强调MDT协作，围术期全程多模式个体化干预：术前评估疼痛敏感性，予对乙酰氨基酚+NSAIDs预防性镇痛提升痛阈，术中实施区域阻滞从源头阻断疼痛信号，术后以按时非阿片类药物为基础，配合静脉自控镇痛泵，按需少量阿片类药物补救镇痛[77,90]。

区域阻滞优先选择覆盖手术区域的超声引导精准阻滞，主要包括胸椎旁阻滞（thoracic paravertebral block, TPVB）、竖脊肌平面阻滞（erector spinae plane block, ESPB）、前锯肌平面阻滞及肋间神经阻滞，均具有良好镇痛效果。胸段硬膜外虽为区域镇痛金标准，但操作复杂、风险较高，已逐渐被精准神经阻滞取代。超声引导区域阻滞效果确切，其中TPVB疗效突出，前锯肌平面阻滞、ESPB、肋间神经阻滞等胸部阻滞疗效非劣于金标准。前锯肌平面阻滞穿刺定位简单，适用于平/侧卧位，针尖受肋骨保护安全性高，可用于复杂疼痛场景的补救镇痛[91]。阿片类药物应用趋向“节俭化”，通过充分区域阻滞及非阿片类背景用药减少用量，降低恶心、呕吐、呼吸抑制等副作用[88]。

个体化是高质量镇痛的另一核心[77]。老年患者肝肾功能可能减退，需调整药物剂量并密切监测呼吸抑制风险；疼痛敏感性高者术前需制定强化多模式镇痛计划，必要时联合疼痛科管理；“无管化”手术（如自主呼吸麻醉）患者需强化区域阻滞，避免影响呼吸。疼痛评估需兼顾静息痛与动态痛，鼓励患者参与评估并根据反馈实时调整方案[92]。

疼痛管理全程SDM至关重要。疼痛管理团队术前需向患者说明术后疼痛的必然性与可控性，介绍拟用镇痛策略及可能感受（如阻滞后胸部麻木），帮助建立合理预期，明确疼痛控制目标是支持早期下床、有效咳嗽，而非完全无痛。双方可共同商定可接受疼痛水平[如数字评分法（numeric rating scale, NRS）≤3分][88]，制定以非阿片类药物为主的出院后镇痛计划。通过持续沟通与动态调整，提升患者康复参与度、治疗依从性及满意度[77]。

推荐意见13：推荐GGO术后疼痛管理采用以超声引导区域阻滞（TPVB等）联合对乙酰氨基酚、NSAIDs等镇痛药为核心的多模式镇痛方案，限制阿片类药物使用。镇痛策略需依据患者年龄、肝肾功能、手术方式及疼痛史个体化调整，由MDT疼痛管理团队协作，医患共同确定疼痛控制目标与康复计划，实现安全、舒适、快速康复（2A）。

5 术后辅助治疗

GGO术后辅助治疗决策是临床难点，核心争议在于精准识别辅助治疗获益人群，避免对惰性病变过度治疗。与传统实性肺癌不同，磨玻璃为主型肺腺癌预后好、复发风险低，辅助治疗需谨慎[4]。AIS、MIA完整切除后几乎无复发风险，共识明确无需辅助治疗[4,9,93]。IAC需结合病理分期、高危因素及分子特征进行个体化判断：IA期无高危因素（如微乳头/实体型成分、脉管癌栓、STAS等）者通常不建议辅助治疗；IA期伴高危病理因素（实性/微乳头/复杂腺体成分≥10%）的表皮生长因子受体（epidermal growth factor receptor, EGFR）突变患者，Zhang等[94]前瞻性APPOINT研究证实，术后予第三代EGFR-酪氨酸激酶抑制剂（tyrosine kinase inhibitors, TKIs）阿美替尼辅助治疗，2年RFS率从75%升至100%，安全性良好，填补该人群治疗空白，为其个体化辅助治疗提供高级别依据；IB期因复发风险存在决策分歧，需综合肿瘤大小、分化程度、胸膜侵犯等评估；II期及以上患者常需辅助治疗，方案依据淋巴结状态及驱动基因突变制定[93,95]。

当前深层争议包括：传统肿瘤原发灶-淋巴结-转移（tumor-node-metastasis, TNM）分期对磨玻璃样肺腺癌的适用性局限、分子残留病灶（minimal residual disease, MRD）检测的潜在价值与临床推广难度[96]，以及驱动基因阳性（如EGFR突变）患者辅助靶向治疗的获益风险比。尤其早期IAC，即便存在敏感突变，仍需慎重权衡低复发风险与靶向治疗的副作用及经济负担[36,97]。此外，多发GGO多为多原发病灶，不同病灶驱动基因可能不同，辅助治疗决策需基于最高分期病灶，而非针对所有GGO[8,41]。

此决策过程充满不确定性，SDM至关重要[98]。医生需全面评估患者复发风险，除病理分期外，还需结合肿瘤CTR、病理亚型、手术切除情况及分子标志物等，向患者清晰说明个体复发风险、辅助治疗的潜在绝对获益、不良反应及经济成本，明确“积极监测”亦是合理选择。尤其临界状态（如IB期、伴高危因素的IA期）患者，需充分沟通了解其复发焦虑程度、治疗副作用耐受性及个人价值取向，共同制定契合意愿与生活质量的方案。MDT讨论为复杂病例决策的重要支撑，整合多学科意见提升决策科学性与全面性[99,100]。

推荐意见14：推荐AIS、MIA患者术后无需辅助治疗（1A）；IA期IAC无高危病理特征或分子异常者，不建议辅助治疗（1B）；IA期IAC行亚肺叶切除，伴实性/微乳头/复杂腺体成分≥10%的 EGFR 突变患者，经MDT充分评估及SDM，全面告知获益、副作用及经济负担后，可谨慎考虑EGFR-TKIs辅助治疗可行性（2B）；IB期患者需综合评估高危因素及基因检测结果，慎重权衡辅助治疗获益与风险（2B）；II期及以上患者，推荐依据淋巴结状态及驱动基因突变选择辅助化疗、靶向治疗或免疫治疗（1A）。所有决策需基于个体复发风险、患者意愿及生活质量诉求，经医患共同参与及MDT讨论制定，避免过度治疗与治疗不足（2A）。鼓励有条件的中心探索MRD等精准医学技术用于复发风险分层，优化辅助治疗策略。

6 术后随访

6.1 随访频率与持续时间的个体化争议

GGO术后随访是长期管理的重要部分，随访频率与持续时间尚无全球统一标准，核心争议是依据个体复发风险制定随访方案，平衡复发监测与过度医疗、心理负担[9,82]。随访频率争议集中于固定间隔与风险分层动态调整[101]。传统指南多建议术后前2年每6个月复查、后续每年1次，但难以体现GGO患者异质性[82]。目前更多观点支持分层管理，高危患者（如IAC伴高危病理亚型、脉管癌栓、STAS或淋巴结阳性）术后早期建议每3-6个月密集随访[102]，低危患者（如AIS、MIA或无高危因素的贴壁样为主型I期癌）复发风险极低，可放宽至每年1次甚至更久[5,36]。

随访持续时间争议围绕终身随访与有限期随访[82]。支持终身随访者认为，肺癌患者始终有第二原发癌风险，部分GGO惰性强，复发可能出现在5年甚至10年后，需长期监测；反对者则认为，AIS、MIA及部分低危I期患者5年后复发概率极低，过度随访成本效益低，还会增加辐射暴露与心理压力，建议5-10年后终止规律影像学随访[36]。此外，随访检查手段选择亦有分歧，LDCT辐射低适用于长期筛查，但部分专家推荐高危患者基线及早期随访用标准剂量CT，以获取更清晰图像便于精准对比[1,82]。

个体化随访方案需综合病理类型、手术情况、高危因素及年龄等[102]。极低危患者（AIS/MIA）可每年随访1次，5年后可商议延长间隔或终止[36]；低危I期患者建议至少随访5年，后期酌情放宽间隔；中高危患者需长期甚至终身定期复查[102]。多发GGO患者随访需基于最高风险已切除病灶制定计划，同时持续监测未处理次病灶[8,59]。年轻低危患者仍建议长期随访，高龄或合并症多者可经充分沟通简化随访流程[102]。

术后随访过程中SDM居核心地位。医生需向患者详细说明随访的双重目的（监测复发、发现第二原发癌）及个体化方案的制定依据（病理结果、手术情况、复发风险层级）[101]，共同权衡不同策略利弊。密集随访可早发现问题，但增加经济与心理负担；宽松随访减轻压力，但可能略增间隔期复发风险（低危患者该风险极低）[101]。同时需尊重患者风险认知与心理承受能力，在医学原则内灵活调整随访计划，并保留根据复查结果动态优化的空间[101]。

推荐意见15：GGO术后随访采用风险分层个体化策略。低危患者（AIS、MIA及无高危因素的贴壁样为主型I期癌）术后1-2年每6-12个月复查，后续每年1次，5年后可酌情延长间隔；中高危患者（伴高危病理特征的I期癌、II期及以上）前2年每3-6个月复查，后续每6-12个月1次，坚持长期随访。所有患者随访以LDCT为主，高危患者基线及早期随访可考虑标准剂量CT。随访计划须经医患充分沟通制定，结合患者临床特点、心理需求及意愿动态调整，平衡科学监测与合理负担（2A）。

6.2 随访检查项目的循证优化

GGO术后随访检查项目优化是临床重要议题，核心是依据个体复发风险制定差异化监测策略，平衡复发发现与过度医疗负担[9,102]。共识支持从传统“一刀切”模式转向基于病理类型、分期及高危因素的个体化随访方案。AIS、MIA患者预后极佳，复发风险极低，随访可简化为年度胸部薄层CT，常规行头颅MRI、全身骨扫描或PET/CT等检出率极低，还会增加不必要辐射、经济成本及心理焦虑[4]。IAC需进一步区分低危与高危：低危者（如IA期无高危因素）建议术后前2年每6个月复查胸部CT，后续过渡至每年1次；高危者（如IB期及以上，或伴脉管癌栓、STAS、实体/微乳头成分等高危特征）需更密切随访，除定期胸部CT外，可酌情行肿瘤标志物监测、头颅MRI或腹部影像学检查，避免无差别全面筛查[5,103]。

确定具体随访项目时需审慎评估各项检查的适用范围与证据支持[9]。PET/CT对磨玻璃为主病灶敏感性有限、代谢活性较低，不推荐作为术后常规随访手段[82]；肿瘤标志物在早期GGO患者中阳性率及特异性均不高，仅作为高危患者辅助参考[82]；支气管镜对周围型无症状病变价值甚微，仅适用于中央型病变或咯血等特定症状时。此外，多发GGO患者随访需基于已切除最高风险病灶的病理结果制定计划，同时长期监测未处理病灶[8,25]。随访全程需兼顾“治愈窗口期”对低危患者的意义，及过度检查的生理心理负担，强调随访精准性与成本效益。

SDM居核心地位。医生需向患者详细说明个体复发风险及随访方案制定依据，包括推荐检查与省略的原因，帮助理解“避免过度”与“足够安全”的平衡[82]。同时明确随访双重目标（监测复发、发现第二原发癌），教育患者识别新发咳嗽、咯血、骨痛、头痛等预警症状，以便及时就医，无需拘泥于固定复查时间[82]。心理焦虑较高或风险认知有差异的患者，需在循证医学原则内尊重其意愿，适当调整随访频率或项目，提升依从性与生活质量[13]。随访计划需保持动态灵活，根据每次复查结果及时调整后续策略[101]。

推荐意见16：推荐GGO术后随访检查项目实施风险分层管理：AIS、MIA患者仅需年度胸部LDCT；低危I期IAC患者术后2年内每6个月行胸部CT，后续每年1次；高危患者（IB期及以上或伴高危因素）需更密切随访，除定期胸部CT外可酌情选择肿瘤标志物、头颅MRI或腹部影像学检查，避免常规应用PET/CT、支气管镜或全身骨扫描。所有随访策略须经医患共同沟通制定，结合患者病理特征、心理需求及意愿动态调整，实现科学监测与合理负担的统一（2A）。

6.3 新发病灶的鉴别与处理策略

GGO术后新发病灶的鉴别与处理是随访常见挑战，需结合影像学特征、动态变化及患者个体情况制定策略[101]。新发病灶可能为良性炎症、第二原发肺癌或转移复发灶，鉴别要点包括病灶密度、边缘形态、增长速度及实性成分变化等[5,21,25]。首次发现、无明显恶性特征的pGGO，建议短期随访（如2-3个月）或经验性抗炎治疗后复查以排除一过性炎症，病灶持续存在但稳定则进入长期密切监测，随访中出现进展（直径增大、实性成分增多）需考虑干预[3,9,21,25]。高度疑似转移或复发灶，应启动MDT讨论，依据全面分期、分子检测结果及患者全身状况制定个体化方案（可能包括手术、靶向治疗、放疗或免疫治疗等）[101,104]。

决策需充分考虑患者肺功能储备、年龄、合并症及心理状态，尤其既往接受肺切除者，再次手术需谨慎评估肺功能耐受性。同时借助薄层CT、人工智能辅助诊断及影像组学等新技术提升鉴别精准度。SDM至关重要，医生需向患者解释新发病灶的多种可能性，阐明随访意图与安全性，帮助理解“治愈窗口期”下惰性病变的适度观察策略，避免过度恐慌导致不必要干预[3,13]。双方共商不同方案利弊，尊重患者对治疗风险与生活质量的权衡，制定符合循证医学原则且体现患者意愿的个体化策略。

推荐意见17：GGO术后新发病灶首选短期随访鉴别良性病变，持续存在但稳定者建议长期影像学监测，进展性病灶可考虑手术切除（优先亚肺叶切除）或其他局部治疗，疑似转移或复发者需经MDT讨论制定系统治疗方案。所有决策基于病灶特征、患者整体状况及意愿，经医患充分沟通制定，保障医疗安全的前提下避免过度诊疗（2A）。

7 总结

GGO诊疗已迈入以“患者价值观为中心”的精准医学新阶段[2,6,7,10]。本共识系统回应GGO管理关键争议，首次将SDM确立为贯穿诊疗全周期的核心原则[12,98]。SDM超越传统医患关系模式，强调在科学证据与个体偏好间寻求平衡，通过结构化对话将不确定性转化为个体化决策契机[98]。

GGO具高度异质性与惰性生物学行为，传统“一刀切”策略过于粗放。本共识明确，从筛查起始年龄、间隔设定，到术中术式选择、术后康复路径，各环节均需融合循证医学与患者生活目标、风险承受力及生命价值观[6,9,16,80]。SDM并非让患者独自抉择，而是医患共建决策伙伴关系：医生提供专业研判与证据解读，患者贡献语境化需求与偏好权重，最终形成契合个体的诊疗方案。

为提升SDM可操作性，建议从以下路径与工具推进实施：一是围绕手术时机、术式等关键决策点开展结构化沟通，系统阐释疾病惰性、“转移成功之前阶段”与“治愈窗口期”概念，结合患者影像特征进行个体化风险分层，清晰说明积极监测、亚肺叶切除、肺叶切除及非手术局部治疗等所有方案的预期获益、风险及对生活质量的长期影响；二是开发应用决策辅助工具，如风险可视化图表（附表1，http://www.lungca.org/files/2025s167s1.xlsx）、选择对比卡片、在线进展概率计算器，配发患者健康教育手册[105]，并将CTR、生长速率等专业术语转化为患者可理解的比喻与日常语言，避免直接呈现原始数据；三是优化沟通流程，推广“问-答-讲”等技巧，鼓励家属参与，可设立SDM咨询门诊保障沟通时间；四是依托MDT支持系统[106]，由护士或个案管理师协助患者教育，探索引入决策教练，医院管理层需将SDM技能培训与实践质量纳入医疗质量评价体系，构建涵盖内容、工具、沟通及系统支持的完整可操作框架。

本共识旨在推动胸外科、呼吸科、肿瘤科等专科在GGO管理中强化协作式、患者价值导向的诊疗模式。预期通过以SDM为核心的结构化沟通与决策流程，提升医疗质量与患者满意度，减少不必要检查治疗及资源浪费，实现医患和谐。

Numerous unresolved controversies persist in current GGO diagnosis and management, spanning the entire disease trajectory: (1) At the diagnostic level: How can lesion invasiveness and aggressiveness be accurately predicted using imaging features—such as size, consolidation-to-tumor ratio (CTR), and morphological evolution? (2) Treatment strategy controversies are especially prominent: For persistent pure GGO (pGGO), should surgical intervention be pursued or active surveillance adopted? If surgery is indicated, when should it be performed [1]? How should surgical modality be selected—sublobar versus lobectomy [2,3]? For multifocal disease, should radical resection or selective management be preferred [4]? How should follow-up intensity, frequency, and duration be determined [5]? No global 'gold standard' exists for these pivotal questions; substantial practice variation exists across academic schools and clinical centers, constituting a 'gray zone' in clinical decision-making. This decisional dilemma is not unique to GGO management but reflects a broader challenge in cancer prevention and control—for instance, individuals carrying pathogenic variants in breast cancer susceptibility genes (BRCA) face analogous trade-offs between early detection/intervention and overtreatment, requiring careful integration of scientific evidence and personal value orientation.

Faced with these controversies, traditional paternalistic or purely technique-oriented models of clinician–patient communication demonstrate clear limitations [2]. Unilateral decision-making by clinicians based solely on guidelines or personal experience—or oversimplifying complex information to elicit passive patient consent—fails to address the high degree of uncertainty, strategic heterogeneity, and outcome variability inherent in GGO management. Patients are not passive recipients; their values, life goals, disease understanding, risk tolerance, quality-of-life expectations, and attitudes toward follow-up burden are all critical determinants of individualized decision-making. Decisions that disregard patient value orientations—even if technically 'correct'—may result in diminished satisfaction, poor adherence, or decisional regret [6].

Therefore, shared decision-making (SDM) holds exceptional value in GGO diagnosis and management—a domain fundamentally characterized by trade-offs. SDM is a structured collaborative process wherein clinicians articulate disease characteristics, systematically present all feasible options, and analyze, using best available evidence, the potential benefits, risks, and long-term implications of each option; patients, after comprehensively understanding the information, express their concerns, life priorities, and value preferences [10]; and both parties jointly reach a decision that aligns with optimal evidence and the patient’s individual circumstances. Integrating SDM into GGO management optimizes decision quality, enhances patients’ sense of autonomy and control, strengthens trust in clinician–patient communication, and improves treatment adherence and follow-up compliance. [11], published in Chest, demonstrated that patients undergoing SDM in lung cancer screening exhibited higher long-term screening adherence: adherence increased by 26.5% in Year 1 and widened to a 32.5% difference by Year 4, suggesting SDM promotes sustained follow-up engagement. SDM provides an actionable decision framework for questions lacking a single definitive answer—such as 'surveillance versus surgery', 'when to operate', and 'which surgical approach'.

The 'Consensus on Shared Decision-Making in the Diagnosis and Management of Ground-Glass Opacity-Featured Lung Adenocarcinoma' was developed by a multidisciplinary expert panel from Shanghai Pulmonary Hospital, Tongji University School of Medicine. Its purpose is to delineate key clinical decision points and contentious scenarios, clarify core SDM elements and implementation workflows, and provide clinically actionable guidance. By promoting SDM, this consensus aims to reduce inter-center and inter-disciplinary practice variation, support structured clinician–patient communication and risk–benefit deliberation under uncertainty, and advance the transformation of clinical practice from 'disease-centered' to 'patient value-centered' care.

1. Screening

1.1 Screening initiation and cessation ages

The initiation and cessation ages for GGO screening constitute a critical clinical issue, and current guidelines lack consensus on recommended screening ages [14]. Controversy surrounding initiation age centers on whether a universal threshold should be established and how to balance screening benefits against associated risks. Some guidelines [1,15] recommend initiating screening at age 45 or 50, whereas other consensus statements [5,16] propose screening for individuals aged ≥40 years with specific risk factors. This indicates that initiation age is not an absolute standard but must be determined through integrated assessment of smoking history, family history, environmental exposures, and genetic background. Particularly for special populations such as never-smoking women, individualized risk assessment—not rigid adherence to age thresholds—is paramount. Clinicians and patients must engage in thorough communication regarding specific risks to jointly deliberate the necessity and timing of screening, ensuring decisions are both evidence-informed and respectful of patient preferences.

Controversy regarding cessation age focuses on balancing screening benefits against potential harms in older adults [17,18]. Although some guidelines designate age 75 as the upper limit for screening cessation, a growing body of opinion supports individualized determination based on overall health status, life expectancy, and treatment tolerance [9,14,17]. Given the higher prevalence of indolent GGOs among elderly patients, continued screening may lead to overdiagnosis and psychological burden; thus, the decision to continue screening requires careful evaluation of comorbidities, expected survival time, and personal preferences [18]. Notably, healthy older adults with prolonged life expectancy should not be denied screening opportunities solely on the basis of advanced age [19]. This complex decision necessitates in-depth clinician–patient dialogue to clarify anticipated benefits and possible risks, enabling co-development of a rational management plan [17,19].

Adolescents and young adults are generally excluded from routine screening unless they harbor clearly defined hereditary high-risk factors; such cases require rigorous evaluation by a multidisciplinary team (MDT) [16]. Throughout the screening decision-making process, SDM plays a central role [13]. Clinicians must elucidate the indolent nature of GGO, the dual nature of screening (benefits vs. harms), and the patient’s individual risk profile, thereby facilitating patient understanding of the rationale and implications of different options [16]; patients, in turn, should openly express their concerns and value orientations. Through mutual trust and collaboration, both parties arrive at a consensus-driven choice. This model is particularly valuable for patients near age thresholds or with ambiguous risk profiles, helping to strike an optimal balance between evidence and preference.

Recommendation 1: For individuals at high risk of lung cancer—including smokers, those with occupational exposures, or those with a family history—we recommend considering LDCT screening starting at age 40 (Grade 2A). For special populations such as never-smoking women, individualized risk assessment incorporating secondhand smoke exposure, kitchen fume exposure, and other relevant factors is advised (Grade 2B). For patients aged ≥75 years, continuation of screening should be determined by integrating health status, life expectancy, and personal preferences—not terminated solely on the basis of advanced age (Grade 2A). All screening decisions must be made through full clinician–patient communication and information sharing, ensuring alignment with both the patient’s best interests and their personal preferences and value orientations (Grade 2A).

1.2 Screening interval

The screening interval for GGOs is a critical component of clinical follow-up; however, no global consensus standard exists. The core controversy lies in balancing "timely detection of lesion progression" against "avoiding excessive examinations, radiation exposure, and psychological burden" [20,21]. Disagreement persists regarding the optimal timing for the first follow-up after initial screening: some advocate short-interval (3–6 month) re-evaluation to exclude transient inflammation—particularly for mixed ground-glass opacities (mGGOs) [8,9]—while others support annual surveillance, arguing that pure GGOs (pGGOs) typically exhibit indolent growth and yield limited benefit from frequent imaging [20-23]. Controversy also surrounds long-term stable GGOs: although some guidelines recommend continued annual screening, emerging evidence supports extending the interval to 1–2 years after stability for ≥2 years—yet all emphasize that surveillance must never be discontinued entirely [5,23,24]. The duration of follow-up should be individualized based on patient age, overall health status, and life expectancy; most consensus statements recommend sustained long-term surveillance for GGOs, as some lesions may progress only years later [23,24].

Clinical determination of the screening interval must integrate radiological features—including GGO type, size, and dynamic changes—alongside patient-specific factors such as risk profile, psychological state, and life expectancy [23,25]. For instance, lesions with substantial solid components, larger diameters, or high-risk clinical features generally warrant shortened follow-up intervals, whereas long-stable, minute pGGOs may justify interval extension [23,25]. Additionally, patients experiencing extreme anxiety may benefit from short-interval follow-up to alleviate distress—though clinicians must remain vigilant against overuse; conversely, elderly patients or those with severe comorbidities should receive more conservative management. Technical consistency is equally critical: standardized institutional protocols and fixed CT acquisition parameters are recommended to ensure comparability across serial examinations. Follow-up scans should employ standard-dose CT to guarantee image clarity and avoid missing subtle evolutionary changes [1]. Radiologists must ensure consistency across key acquisition and reconstruction parameters—including slice thickness, radiation dose, and reconstruction algorithm—to enable precise, reproducible quantitative measurement and longitudinal comparison of GGOs, thereby providing reliable technical support for subsequent clinical decision-making.

Shared decision-making (SDM) plays a central role in this domain. Clinicians should proactively explain to patients the indolent nature of GGOs and the dual purpose of surveillance—clarifying both the value of timely follow-up for early detection of progression and the potential risks of repeated radiation exposure and psychological stress [3]. When formulating a surveillance plan, clinicians must engage patients in individualized discussions that incorporate specific lesion characteristics, risk stratification, psychological resilience, and personal preferences to jointly determine the initial interval and long-term strategy. A study by Chung et al. [13] further demonstrated that patients undergoing SDM in lung cancer screening exhibited significantly higher long-term adherence to screening programs, with benefits sustained over multiple years—providing robust empirical support for the pivotal role of SDM in long-term GGO surveillance management. Surveillance strategies must be reassessed and dynamically adjusted after each follow-up examination, particularly for patients whose optimal interval falls within a 'gray zone' (e.g., long-stable GGOs or those with discordant age and life expectancy), necessitating thorough communication and consensus-building to embody the principle of 'patient-centered care.'

Recommendation 2: Annual surveillance is recommended for pGGOs <5 mm; after confirmed stability, the interval may be extended to 1–2 years (2A). For newly detected or pGGOs ≥5 mm in diameter, the first follow-up should occur at 6–12 months (2A). For mGGOs or lesions with solid components ≥5 mm, the follow-up interval should be shortened to 3–6 months (2A). All long-stable GGOs require continued surveillance; although the interval may be moderately extended, termination of surveillance is not advised (2A). Ultimately, the surveillance schedule must be individualized based on GGO characteristics, patient-specific risk factors, psychological status, and life expectancy—and finalized through comprehensive clinician–patient communication and shared decision-making (2A).

2 Diagnosis

2.1 Limitations of Imaging-Based Diagnosis

Although imaging-based diagnosis of GGOs serves as a cornerstone for clinical decision-making, its intrinsic limitations substantially influence therapeutic strategy formulation and clinician–patient communication. High-resolution computed tomography (HRCT) remains the primary imaging modality for lesion detection and longitudinal surveillance; yet it cannot substitute for histopathological diagnosis—a fundamental limitation that renders qualitative interpretation inherently ambiguous [5]. The same GGO appearance on HRCT may correspond to early-stage lung adenocarcinoma or to benign conditions such as inflammation, hemorrhage, or fibrosis, rendering definitive characterization difficult upon initial detection. This ambiguity fuels controversy regarding immediate intervention versus short-term surveillance and contributes to patient anxiety and unnecessary radiation exposure [9,18].

Even when imaging findings strongly suggest malignancy, prediction of histopathological subtype remains subject to considerable error—especially in accurately distinguishing atypical adenomatous hyperplasia (AAH), adenocarcinoma in situ (AIS), and minimally invasive adenocarcinoma (MIA); moreover, the actual degree of invasion is frequently underestimated. Recent studies [26] indicate that quantitative CT attenuation analysis can partially assist in staging GGO pathology: for example, a mean CT value >−472 HU often correlates with invasive adenocarcinoma (IAC), whereas lower values are more typical of preinvasive lesions. However, CT values are influenced by scanner hardware, reconstruction algorithms, and measurement methodology; standardized thresholds remain lacking, and clinical interpretation thus requires caution. Upstaging at final surgical pathology occurs not infrequently, directly impacting decisions regarding resection extent and lymph node dissection—thereby becoming a focal point of intraoperative and postoperative management controversies. Furthermore, imaging has limited predictive capacity for biological behaviors such as GGO growth rate and invasive/metastatic potential, leaving significant assessment blind spots. Positron emission tomography (PET)/CT demonstrates limited diagnostic utility in GGOs with scant solid components and is prone to false-negative results in nodal assessment—leading to clinical staging uncertainty. Nevertheless, PET/CT retains important metabolic information in specific scenarios, including suspected intrapulmonary metastasis among multifocal GGOs or evaluation of postoperative suspicious recurrence.

Radiological assessment during GGO surveillance likewise faces challenges related to measurement variability and subjective interpretation: quantification of size, density, and solid component evolution lacks absolute objective standards, and interobserver or interscan discrepancies may arise—directly affecting the definition of 'progression' and subsequent management adjustments. For multifocal GGOs, imaging provides limited discriminatory power in differentiating synchronous primary tumors from intrapulmonary metastases or in identifying the dominant lesion, necessitating supplementary molecular pathological techniques and thereby increasing clinical decision complexity [27]. In light of these limitations, SDM assumes particular importance [13]. Clinicians should proactively inform patients about the inherent uncertainties in imaging interpretations—including probabilistic estimates of pathological stage and the risk of misclassification during surveillance—so that patients understand the evidentiary basis and trade-offs underlying treatment choices. Radiologists, within multidisciplinary team (MDT) evaluations, bear responsibilities beyond descriptive reporting: they should provide preliminary assessments of GGO nature (e.g., probability of benignity, likelihood of invasion) and progression risk, integrating HRCT features (size, density, morphology, dynamic evolution), quantitative CT attenuation analysis, and qualitative evaluation—thereby delivering direct, actionable radiological input for clinical decision-making.

When formulating individualized management plans, clinicians must synthesize clinical, radiological, and—if available—histopathological data. For complex cases, MDT consultation is strongly recommended to compensate for imaging limitations [28]. Concurrently, clinicians should remain cognizant of emerging technologies—including radiomics and artificial intelligence—that hold promise for enhancing objectivity and predictive accuracy in GGO diagnosis and prognosis, though their clinical application remains investigational.

Recommendation 3: Clinicians are advised to explicitly acknowledge the limitations of imaging in qualitative diagnosis, histopathological grading, and prediction of biological behavior when evaluating GGOs—avoiding definitive conclusions based solely on imaging findings (2A). Quantitative metrics such as CT attenuation values may be used adjunctively but require careful interpretation due to methodological variability and inherent constraints (2B). In clinical decision-making, clinicians must thoroughly discuss with patients the uncertainties inherent in imaging results and their implications for subsequent management, jointly weighing the risks and benefits of alternative strategies (2A). For diagnostically challenging cases, MDT integration of multimodal information is recommended to improve diagnostic accuracy and therapeutic appropriateness; all decisions must reflect patient participation and individualization principles (2A).

2.2 Necessity and Risks of Invasive Diagnostic Procedures

Whether invasive diagnostic procedures—such as percutaneous transthoracic needle biopsy or bronchoscopic biopsy—are indicated for GGOs remains highly controversial, centering on the balance between the benefits of definitive histopathological diagnosis and the procedural risks and downstream therapeutic implications [25]. For solitary GGOs amenable to surgical resection, most experts favor direct surgical excision over preoperative biopsy, given its dual diagnostic and therapeutic function, high procedural efficiency, and avoidance of biopsy-related complications—including false-negative results, sampling error, pneumothorax, hemorrhage, and rare needle-track implantation [2,9,29-32]. Notably, GGO biopsy poses unique technical challenges: low lesion density and soft texture, frequent alveolar collapse or fibrotic stroma, difficulty obtaining representative tissue samples, blurred lesion boundaries, and substantial respiratory motion—all contributing to higher false-negative rates (reported at 10%–30%) compared with solid nodules. Moreover, biopsy-induced pleural adhesions or local imaging artifacts may complicate subsequent surgical planning or interfere with lesion assessment.

Nonetheless, invasive diagnosis retains important value in specific clinical contexts [33]. When imaging features are atypical or benign disease (e.g., inflammation, infection) cannot be confidently excluded, biopsy may provide useful diagnostic information. It is also necessary for patients who are elderly, medically frail, or otherwise unable to tolerate surgery—or for those who firmly decline surgery but require histopathological guidance for non-surgical therapies (e.g., radiotherapy, ablation) [9,33]. Furthermore, in multifocal GGOs, biopsy of lesions exhibiting atypical or aggressive imaging features can clarify histopathological subtype to guide overall therapeutic strategy [8]; and tissue acquisition prior to neoadjuvant therapy is essential for molecular profiling [34]. Prior to biopsy, careful risk–benefit assessment is mandatory: small, purely ground-glass lesions or those located deep within the lung parenchyma or adjacent to major vessels or vital structures confer low diagnostic yield relative to procedural risk and thus warrant heightened caution.

SDM plays a central role in this decision process [13]. Clinicians should clearly explain the indolent nature of GGOs and objectively articulate the advantages and disadvantages of direct surgical resection versus upfront biopsy: the former offers high efficiency but entails surgical morbidity and potential overtreatment; the latter may avert unnecessary surgery yet carries procedural complications, elevated false-negative risk (particularly for pGGOs), and treatment delay [35]. Assessment must comprehensively consider GGO biopsiability (size, location, proximity to vessels), the planned biopsy technique (e.g., coaxial CT-guided biopsy improves tissue yield and reduces pleural puncture次数), operator expertise, and the pathology laboratory’s diagnostic capability for scant biopsy specimens. Technically, thin-section CT should be used for precise path planning; fine-needle or core biopsy needles are preferred; and close attention to patient respiratory coordination during the procedure maximizes diagnostic yield and minimizes risk. Patient-specific factors—including overall health status and personal risk tolerance—must also be integrated, with full respect accorded to individual preferences regarding either definitive diagnosis or expedited treatment [25]; especially in borderline cases or for patients with low tolerance for uncertainty, thorough communication is essential to reach an individualized, mutually agreed decision [10,13].

Recommendation 4: For solitary GGOs highly suspicious for malignancy and surgically resectable, direct surgical resection is preferred to avoid the additional risks and diagnostic–therapeutic delays associated with invasive diagnostic procedures (2A). Invasive diagnosis is recommended when surgery is contraindicated, declined by the patient, or required to guide therapeutic strategy for multifocal disease (2A). All decisions must be individualized based on comprehensive assessment of lesion characteristics, patient health status, and preferences—and finalized through thorough clinician–patient communication and shared decision-making (2A).

3 Treatment

3.1 Surgical Indications and Timing

Surgical indications and timing for GGOs represent a core area of controversy, hinging on identification of the 'pre-metastatic window'—the biological evolutionary stage before the lesion acquires local or distant metastatic capacity, clinically approximated by achieving a 10-year recurrence-free survival (RFS) rate of 100%. Optimal management thus requires a rational balance between proactive intervention and active surveillance. Some advocate prompt surgical resection for GGOs highly suspicious for malignancy to mitigate progression risk and patient anxiety. Li et al. [36] demonstrated in a 10-year postoperative survival study of pure ground-glass adenocarcinoma that pGGO patients achieved a 10-year RFS rate of 100%, with no recurrences even among those with invasive adenocarcinoma (IAC) pathology—supporting classification of pGGO as a surgically curable, indolent subtype. Longitudinal follow-up studies [3] tracking 135 pGGOs (median follow-up: 193 months) revealed that even after 10 years of stability, 3.9% eventually progressed—indicating that indolence does not equate to zero progression risk. Yet mounting evidence [37] confirms that most pGGOs and mGGOs with CTR ≤0.25 exhibit marked indolence, existing in an 'immune equilibrium' state with extremely low metastatic success rates. Li et al. [38] reported that for sub-solid pulmonary GGOs ≤2 cm with CTR ≤0.25, the observation group achieved a 5-year event-free survival rate of 100%, demonstrating non-inferiority (P<0.001) versus the surgical group (99.0%), supporting conservative management for this population. Another prospective study [39] following 1003 GGOs (684 patients) for over 10 years further confirmed no significant difference in 10-year overall survival between surveillance and surgical groups—regardless of whether GGOs remained stable or enlarged. Both groups showed identical long-term survival outcomes, with 7-year RFS rates of 100% for both stable and enlarging GGOs; notably, GGO enlargement did not increase surgical cost or operative duration. This study definitively establishes CT surveillance as an appropriate management strategy for GGOs until emergence of solid components. AIS, MIA, and IAC with CTR = 0 achieve near-100% 10-year RFS after resection—representing the prototypical 'pre-metastatic window'; even if progression occurs during surveillance and triggers delayed surgery, patients retain exceptionally high cure rates, indicating a sufficiently wide 'therapeutic window' for individualized decision-making [36].

International and domestic guidelines exhibit marked divergence in GGO management, reflecting differing national perspectives on GGO biology [25,40]. The Chinese Expert Consensus on Pulmonary Nodule Management recommends surgical consideration for pGGOs 5–10 mm in diameter once they enlarge to >10 mm [9,41]; Japanese guidelines raise this threshold to 15 mm; the U.S. National Comprehensive Cancer Network (NCCN) guidelines recommend resection only for persistently present pGGOs ≥20 mm; and certain European guidelines extend the threshold to 30 mm [40]. These differences stem from variations in ethnic characteristics, healthcare resources, health-economic considerations, and assessments of pGGO invasiveness. Importantly, the prevalence of MIA and IAC among radiologically defined pGGOs is non-negligible: a retrospective study of 273 IAC cases at Shanghai Pulmonary Hospital [42] suggests that for denser pGGOs—even those <10 mm in diameter—clinicians should maintain high vigilance for invasive potential. Hu et al. [43] demonstrated that a multiparametric predictive model incorporating pGGO size, maximum CT value, and morphological features (lobulation, spiculation, pleural indentation, bubble-like lucency, and vascular convergence) significantly improves preoperative diagnostic accuracy for IAC, thereby enabling more precise timing of surgical intervention.

Should persistently present pGGOs trigger surgery upon volumetric enlargement, or should intervention await emergence of solid components? Proponents of earlier surgery argue that persistent growth or increasing solid components may signal enhanced invasiveness [44]. Studies [6,36] show that the 10-year RFS rate for pGGOs in the surgical cohort is 100%, while recurrence risk rises significantly with increasing CTR. Among patients with CTR >0.50 and tumor size >1 cm, the postoperative recurrence rate reaches 16.22%, with most recurrences occurring within 5 years—particularly for peripheral lesions amenable to sublobar resection—suggesting that early intervention may achieve definitive cure with minimal morbidity. Conversely, advocates of cautious surveillance emphasize that emergence of solid components represents a more reliable marker of increased invasiveness, and premature surgery risks overtreatment. Notably, the risk of spread through air spaces (STAS) increases with rising CTR, with a marked elevation observed at CTR >0.50 [44]; STAS has even been documented in 2%–11% of pGGOs, underscoring their biological heterogeneity and precluding simplistic assumptions of 'zero progression risk.' Therefore, peripheral GGOs with high imaging suspicion for invasion warrant more aggressive intervention; for centrally located lesions requiring lobectomy, close surveillance until solid component emergence (CTR ≤0.25) is reasonable—but intervention must occur prior to nodal metastasis. Analysis of 86 patients with 0.25 < CTR ≤0.50 in the JCOG0201 study [45] revealed elevated nodal metastasis risk, supporting more proactive management for mGGOs with CTR >0.25. The European Association for Cardio-thoracic Surgery (EACTS)/European Society of Thoracic Surgeons (ESTS) guidelines [40] emphasize that decisions should be grounded in MDT evaluation, integrating GGO dynamics, solid component evolution, and patient-specific risk factors.

Multiple factors influence surgical decision-making: GGO imaging features are paramount—including diameter, CTR, and dynamic evolution [25]. Larger diameter (e.g., >8 mm), CTR >0.25, or progressive change on surveillance generally favor surgical intervention [25,44]. Indeed, the aforementioned studies in stage IA invasive NSCLC [44] confirm CTR >0.50 as a significant predictor of recurrence; deep lobulation and elevated carcinoembryonic antigen (CEA) levels are also independent adverse prognostic factors meriting comprehensive assessment [46]. Patient age, life expectancy, and psychological status are equally critical: younger patients may undergo elective intervention, whereas older or comorbid patients are better suited for surveillance; patients with anxiety severely impairing quality of life may opt for surgery after thorough informed consent [25]. Estimated pathological stage directly shapes strategy: the 2018 'Shanghai Pulmonary Hospital Consensus on Early Lung Adenocarcinoma Manifesting as Ground-Glass Nodules (First Edition)' [4,26] proposes that for pGGOs >8 mm with high suspicion for AIS, surgery may be considered if psychological distress is substantial; for MIA and IAC, surgery is recommended (MIA achieves near-100% cure postoperatively; even GGO-predominant IAC carries a small but non-negligible risk of high-grade subtypes or recurrence) [4,26]. For multifocal pGGOs, the principle of 'treating the dominant lesion while surveilling secondary lesions' is commonly applied [8,25,41].

SDM occupies a central position in this complex decision-making process. Clinicians must clearly explain concepts such as GGO indolence, the 'pre-metastatic window,' and the 'therapeutic window'—and, supported by evidence, convey that lesions typically progress slowly, affording ample time for deliberation [3,36]. They must objectively outline the potential benefits and risks of surgery versus the significance and burdens of surveillance, engaging patients in thorough discussion of risk–benefit trade-offs across alternative management options [25]. Final decisions must be anchored in individual patient circumstances—including GGO behavior, age, health status, psychological resilience, and personal values—and reached through comprehensive dialogue and consensus [25,40]. For borderline cases (e.g., CTR ≈0.25), in-depth clinician–patient exchange and collaborative choice are especially crucial [44]. Decisions should retain appropriate flexibility and respect fully informed patient preferences. When choosing between 'surgery' and 'surveillance,' the essence is balancing 'early diagnosis and treatment' against 'avoiding overtreatment'—making an individualized choice between oncologic curability and long-term management strategy [36,40].

Recommendation 5: (1) For persistent mGGOs with larger diameter (typically >8 mm), higher solid component proportion (CTR >0.25), or progression on surveillance (volumetric enlargement or increased solid component), and with high suspicion for MIA or IAC, biopsy or surgical resection is recommended to establish definitive diagnosis (1B). (2) For pGGOs ≥10 mm showing progression (volumetric enlargement or increased density) on surveillance, or exhibiting malignant imaging features—including bubble-like lucency (cystic cavity), lobulation, spiculation, air-bronchogram, pleural indentation, or vascular convergence—and located peripherally or within a single pulmonary segment (thus amenable to sublobar resection), biopsy or surgical resection is favored to confirm diagnosis. If the lesion is centrally located and would necessitate lobectomy, surveillance may continue until solid component emergence (CTR ≤0.25). Persistently present pGGOs ≥15 mm (present for ≥3–6 months) warrant strong consideration for biopsy or surgical resection (1B). (3) For pGGOs <10 mm and mGGOs <8 mm: ① If AIS is highly suspected, surveillance is prioritized; however, if clear changes emerge—including diameter increase >2 mm, marked density elevation, or increased solid component—follow-up interval should be shortened or biopsy/surgery pursued for definitive diagnosis; ② If MIA or IAC is highly suspected, multidisciplinary team (MDT) discussion is recommended, followed by clinician–patient co-development of an individualized plan; if patient anxiety is severe, biopsy or surgical indications may be appropriately liberalized (2A). (4) All decisions must be individualized, integrating patient age, life expectancy, health status, psychological needs, estimated pathological stage, lesion location, and other relevant factors—and finalized through comprehensive communication and shared decision-making (SDM). Patient preferences must be respected, and decisional flexibility preserved. Under guaranteed oncologic safety, preference should be given to less morbid, lung-function–preserving strategies to avoid overtreatment (2A).

3.2 Surgical Approach Selection

3.2.1 Controversy Regarding Resection Extent: Individualized Strategies for Sublobar versus Lobectomy and Lymph Node Management The surgical management strategy for ground-glass opacity (GGO) lesions represents a core issue in thoracic surgery, wherein resection extent and lymph node management must be considered as an integrated decision rather than isolated components [40]. Contemporary GGO management philosophy emphasizes individualized, concurrent determination of resection extent and lymph node handling—based on tumor biological behavior—to achieve optimal balance among oncologic radicality, pulmonary functional preservation, and immune function maintenance [9,37].

Lobectomy combined with systematic mediastinal lymph node dissection remains the traditional standard procedure, indicated for lesions with substantial solid components, high invasive potential, or suspected lymph node metastasis [25]. However, for GGO-predominant lung adenocarcinoma—which exhibits distinct biological behavior—this standard warrants re-evaluation. Evidence confirms that pure GGO (pGGO) lesions and part-solid GGOs with a consolidation-to-tumor ratio (CTR) ≤0.25 harbor extremely low rates of lymph node metastasis and demonstrate excellent prognosis. The JCOG0804 study [47] reported a 5-year recurrence-free survival (RFS) rate of 99.7% following sublobar resection for lesions ≤2 cm in diameter with CTR ≤0.25. The JCOG0802/WJOG4607L trial [6] demonstrated non-inferior overall survival (OS) for anatomical segmentectomy versus lobectomy in peripheral non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) ≤2 cm with CTR >0.50. Similarly, the North American CALGB140503 trial [7] confirmed no significant difference in OS between sublobar resection and lobectomy for peripheral NSCLC ≤2 cm. Collectively, these studies provide robust evidence supporting the application of sublobar resection [31].

However, real-world studies present a more complex clinical picture: A large-scale analysis from the Society of Thoracic Surgeons database, encompassing 32,340 patients with clinical stage IA (≤2 cm) NSCLC, revealed significantly superior overall survival (OS) and lung cancer-specific survival (LCSS) for lobectomy and segmentectomy compared with wedge resection—the disparity potentially attributable to inadequate lymph node assessment in wedge resection (median sampling only three nodes; Nx rate 20.8%) [48]. A retrospective study at Asan Medical Center in Seoul, involving 1,205 patients with clinical stage IA lung adenocarcinoma, found no significant difference in RFS between wedge and segmentectomy—provided adequate negative margins (≥1 cm or ≥tumor diameter for cT1a; ≥2 cm or ≥tumor diameter for cT1b) and sufficient lymph node evaluation (≥3 N2 stations plus ≥1 N1 station); 5-year RFS was 92.4% versus 93.3%, respectively [49]. These findings underscore that, regardless of resection modality, achieving adequate surgical margins and performing systematic lymph node assessment are paramount.

Lymph node management strategies must be individualized concurrently with resection extent. An interim analysis of the phase III randomized controlled trial ECTOP-1009 (NCT04527419) by Zhang et al. [37] demonstrated no nodal metastases in patients with GGO-predominant invasive adenocarcinoma (IAC) and CTR ≤0.50 undergoing systematic mediastinal lymph node dissection; moreover, the dissection group exhibited significantly longer operative time, greater intraoperative blood loss, prolonged postoperative hospital stay, and serious complications including chylothorax and intraoperative massive hemorrhage—suggesting that routine systematic dissection confers no survival benefit while increasing procedural morbidity and complication risk in this population. A multicenter retrospective study (GORDON study) [50] enrolling 400 surgically resected pGGOs employed receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve analysis to identify radiological predictors of invasiveness: combined diameter ≥18 mm and CT value >−390 HU achieved 81.2% predictive accuracy (area under the curve [AUC] = 0.874), offering guidance for preoperative risk stratification and surgical planning. Crucially, the core finding of this study was that none of the enrolled pGGO patients—including 346 with IAC—exhibited lymph node metastasis, yielding a 5-year nodal RFS of 100%. This indicates that pGGOs, irrespective of high-risk radiological features, do not require routine systematic lymph node dissection. Furthermore, lymph nodes serve as critical sites for T-cell activation and immune regulation; a 2023 Cell study [51] reported that therapy-responsive T cells predominantly originate from lymph nodes. Excessive lymph node dissection may therefore impair antitumor immune responses. Hence, in the absence of metastatic evidence, unnecessary dissection should be avoided whenever oncologically safe—balancing metastatic control against immune function preservation.

Based on the above evidence, clinical decision-making adheres to the following principles: For GGOs ≤2 cm in diameter with CTR ≤0.25, sublobar resection (wedge or segmentectomy) without systematic lymph node dissection—or with sampling only—is reasonable [37]; for centrally located lesions, three-dimensional reconstruction is recommended to ensure adequate negative margins (typically ≥2 cm or ≥tumor diameter) [40]. For lesions ≤2 cm with CTR >0.50, anatomical segmentectomy is recommended as one standard option; however, given the 10–20% prevalence of high-risk histopathological subtypes (e.g., micropapillary/solid components >5%) or spread through air spaces (STAS), extended resection and broader lymph node dissection may be warranted [6]. Intraoperative frozen section (FS) diagnostic accuracy is pivotal for integrated decision-making: FS diagnosis of atypical adenomatous hyperplasia (AAH), adenocarcinoma in situ (AIS), or minimally invasive adenocarcinoma (MIA) supports sublobar resection without lymph node dissection or with sampling only; lepidic-predominant IAC with imaging-based CTR ≤0.50 permits sublobar resection combined with lobe-specific lymph node dissection or sampling; high-risk subtype IAC or CTR >0.50 mandates lobectomy with systematic lymph node dissection. For multiple GGOs, resection extent and lymph node strategy are determined by the pathological result of the dominant lesion.

Shared decision-making (SDM) is central to this comprehensive decision process. Physicians must comprehensively inform patients about the core advantages and disadvantages of each surgical approach: lobectomy offers strong oncologic radicality but entails greater trauma and significant impact on pulmonary function; sublobar resection preserves more lung parenchyma, thereby enhancing quality of life and facilitating management of multifocal disease, albeit with a marginally higher risk of local recurrence [6,7]. Concurrently, patient preferences, expectations regarding quality of life, and tolerance for recurrence risk must be individually discussed. All decisions must integrate preoperative high-resolution CT assessment, intraoperative FS results, and patient-specific factors—including pulmonary function, age, and comorbidities—and be formulated through thorough physician–patient communication. It is recommended that individualized surgery be performed at medical centers equipped with three-dimensional reconstruction capabilities, video-assisted thoracoscopy/robotic platforms, and rapid intraoperative pathological diagnosis, with enhanced multidisciplinary team (MDT) collaboration to mitigate decision uncertainty.

Recommendation 6: For GGOs ≤2 cm in diameter, wedge or segmentectomy is appropriate when CTR ≤0.25; segmentectomy is recommended when 0.25 < CTR ≤0.50, with selective lymph node dissection or sampling only (1B). For lesions ≤2 cm with CTR >0.50, anatomical segmentectomy or lobectomy is recommended; lymph node management is guided by intraoperative FS: high-risk histopathological subtypes or CTR >0.75 warrant lobectomy with systematic lymph node dissection; lepidic-predominant IAC with CTR 0.50–0.75 permits segmentectomy with lobe-specific lymph node dissection. For lesions ≤2 cm with CTR >0.50 located directly beneath the pleura (edge distance <1 cm), wedge resection may be considered as an alternative in patients with insufficient pulmonary reserve or multiple comorbidities—provided adequate margins can be technically ensured—but requires sampling of at least three mediastinal lymph node stations (1B). For larger lesions (>2 cm), those with abundant solid components (CTR >0.50), deep-seated or centrally located lesions, or those with suspected lymph node metastasis, lobectomy with systematic lymph node dissection is prioritized (1A). All surgical plans must be jointly formulated by physicians and patients after thorough discussion, integrating preoperative CT assessment, intraoperative FS findings, and individual patient circumstances (2A).

3.2.2 Optimization of Minimally Invasive Approaches: Technical Selection and Cost–Effectiveness Minimally invasive surgery for GGOs currently centers on video-assisted thoracoscopy, with several modalities coexisting: uniportal video-assisted thoracoscopic surgery (U-VATS), robot-assisted thoracic surgery (RATS), and multiportal video-assisted thoracoscopic surgery (M-VATS)—each possessing distinct technical characteristics and clinical applicability [52,53]. U-VATS is now mature, featuring minimal single-incision trauma and superior cosmetic outcomes; it is suitable for most GGO procedures, particularly wedge resection or simple segmentectomy of peripheral lesions. For complex anatomical segmentectomies, experienced surgeons may consider U-VATS, though technical difficulty is high and instrument interference common—thus demanding considerable operator expertise [54,55]. RATS leverages advantages including three-dimensional high-definition visualization, flexible instrument articulation, and tremor filtration, excelling in complex segmentectomies and lymph node dissections; its drawbacks include high equipment and procedural costs and lack of tactile feedback [52,56]. M-VATS is the most widely applied and technically mature modality, offering stable operation and broad adaptability across most thoracoscopic procedures, though its multiple incisions confer slightly inferior postoperative pain control and cosmesis relative to single-port approaches [55,57].

Selection of a specific surgical modality requires integration of multiple factors: lesion characteristics and procedural complexity are paramount—simple resections may be performed via U-VATS or M-VATS based on surgeon proficiency and patient preference, whereas complex anatomical resections are better suited to RATS or M-VATS [58]. Surgeon familiarity with each technique is decisive: U-VATS has a steep learning curve; RATS requires systematic training; M-VATS enjoys the highest technical dissemination [53]. Additionally, patient preferences—including postoperative pain control, recovery speed, cosmetic concerns, and financial capacity—must be weighed: younger or cost-sensitive patients may favor U-VATS or M-VATS, while some may accept RATS for enhanced precision [56]. Hospital infrastructure also constrains choice: RATS necessitates advanced robotic systems, unavailable at all institutions.

SDM occupies a central role throughout this decision process. Physicians must objectively and comprehensively inform patients about the advantages and disadvantages of each modality—including incision size, pain intensity, recovery duration, cost differentials, and context-specific suitability—and provide professional recommendations grounded in personal experience. Simultaneously, patient preferences and values—including expectations for minimally invasive treatment, economic considerations, and trade-offs between safety and efficacy—must be fully respected. The ultimate goal is to collaboratively determine the optimal surgical plan by integrating lesion characteristics, patient physiology, psychological needs, and available healthcare resources—maximizing oncologic radicality while minimizing trauma, accelerating recovery, and enhancing therapeutic experience [10,54].

Recommendation 7: Minimally invasive surgical modality selection for GGOs should be individualized based on lesion location, size, complexity, and patient-specific factors: U-VATS technology is now mature and applicable to most GGO surgeries; U-VATS is preferred for small peripheral lesions or straightforward procedures; RATS or M-VATS is recommended for complex anatomical resections, though experienced surgeons may opt for U-VATS; final decisions must integrate institutional equipment availability, surgeon technical expertise, and patient preferences regarding trauma, recovery, and cost—formulated through thorough physician–patient communication (2A). All procedures must prioritize oncologic radicality while maximizing minimally invasive benefits and expediting recovery.

3.3 Management Strategy for Multiple GGOs

Management of multiple GGOs constitutes a clinical challenge and hotspot in thoracic surgery, centered on accurately distinguishing multifocal primary lung cancer from intrapulmonary metastasis. On this basis, individualized treatment plans must be developed to maximize pulmonary functional preservation while ensuring oncologic curability [8,25,27]. A multicenter cohort study by Kim et al. [59] involving 409 patients who underwent surgical resection for subsolid lung adenocarcinoma identified 1,791 synchronous subsolid GGOs; with a median follow-up of 80.6 months, only 16.9% demonstrated growth, and 7.9% were ultimately diagnosed as lung cancer—all stage 0–I adenocarcinomas—without significantly impacting overall survival. The study further noted that GGO progression correlates primarily with intrinsic radiological features (e.g., part-solid morphology, larger diameter, bubble-like lucency, or pleural traction), not lesion number. This aligns with the consensus statement 'Multidisciplinary Diagnosis and Treatment Expert Consensus on Multifocal Ground-Glass Nodule-Like Lung Cancer (2024 Edition)' [8], which posits that multifocal GGO-like lung cancer represents a distinct subtype of multifocal primary lung cancer, wherein each nodule behaves as an 'independent entity' rather than a metastatic deposit—conferring favorable prognosis, with overall outcome dictated by the dominant lesion [41].

Clinically, initial differentiation relies primarily on high-resolution CT features—including morphology, density, distribution, and dynamic changes. For instance, GGOs located in different lobes, exhibiting heterogeneous morphologies, or demonstrating discordant growth rates strongly suggest multifocal primary disease; however, definitive diagnosis requires molecular pathological confirmation. Although next-generation sequencing provides valuable insights, its clinical adoption remains limited due to cost and tumor heterogeneity. Absence of shared driver gene mutations across resected or biopsied lesions constitutes one diagnostic criterion for multifocal GGO-like lung cancer.

Notably, the detection rate of multifocal GGOs in China has reached 20–40.5%, with a trend toward younger onset; the detection rate among individuals aged ≥70 years is 35–37.52%. Beyond imaging assessment, laboratory parameters—including mitochondrial-related enzymes [superoxide dismutase (SOD), aspartate aminotransferase (AST)], cytokines [interleukin-6 (IL-6), tumor necrosis factor-α (TNF-α)], immunological markers [CD4/CD8 ratio, natural killer (NK) cell count], and tumor biomarkers [carcinoembryonic antigen (CEA), cytokeratin 19 fragment (CYFRA21-1)]—may be integrated to improve diagnostic accuracy.

Therapeutic strategy has shifted from 'radical resection' to 'effective disease control under pulmonary functional preservation' [25]. Kim et al.'s study [59] supports this paradigm, noting that synchronous GGO growth does not significantly affect survival; thus, the dominant lesion typically warrants active intervention—segmentectomy or wedge resection selected according to size, CTR, and location to preserve healthy lung tissue—with lobectomy reserved only when the dominant lesion meets lobectomy criteria. Intervention thresholds for pGGOs may be appropriately tightened—for example, raising the threshold to 15 mm [41]. Secondary lesions are managed primarily via regular surveillance, with intervention deferred until documented progression. Synchronous or staged surgery must weigh lesion distribution, pulmonary functional reserve, and patient tolerance: bilateral lesions—particularly in patients with compromised pulmonary reserve—are better managed with staged procedures to avoid severe postoperative pulmonary dysfunction, limiting total resected lung segments to ≤10 [41]. This concurs with the surgical principle outlined in the 'Multidisciplinary Diagnosis and Treatment Expert Consensus on Multifocal Ground-Glass Nodule-Like Lung Cancer (2024 Edition)' [8]: priority management of the dominant lesion while addressing secondary lesions, aiming for synchronous resection of all lesions where oncologically sound and functionally feasible—but most GGOs need not be resected. Dominant lesions are primarily treated with lobectomy or segmentectomy; secondary lesions undergo segmentectomy or wedge resection; ipsilateral secondary lesions located in the outer two-thirds of the lung may be synchronously resected via sublobar approaches, whereas those in the inner one-third, contralateral lesions, or lesions with solid component <5 mm or CTR <0.25 may be managed via close surveillance or thermal ablation [41]. Regarding lymph node management, GGO-predominant lesions typically warrant sampling or lobe-specific dissection of the dominant lesion only—not routine systematic dissection [37]. Preoperative MDT discussion—integrating multidisciplinary expert opinions, artificial intelligence–assisted assessment, and patient preferences—is recommended to formulate the optimal plan [8].

Non-surgical therapies are gaining prominence in multifocal GGO management, particularly thermal ablation techniques, which offer distinct advantages. Stereotactic body radiation therapy (SBRT) and ablation provide viable alternatives for patients unable to tolerate surgery, those with progressive secondary lesions, or those declining repeat surgery. The 'Multidisciplinary Diagnosis and Treatment Expert Consensus on Multifocal Ground-Glass Nodule-Like Lung Cancer (2024 Edition)' [8] identifies image-guided thermal ablation (IGTA) as one first-line therapeutic option for multifocal GGO-like lung cancer—or as a crucial adjunct to surgical resection, with hybrid surgery (surgery + IGTA) emerging as a novel modality [41,60]. However, its use for secondary lesions in operable patients remains controversial and requires strict adherence to indications, incorporating lesion size, location, and histopathological type into comprehensive judgment [41]. Moreover, non-invasive assessment technologies—including circulating tumor DNA (ctDNA) and exosomal microRNA—hold promise for future risk stratification and treatment response monitoring.

SDM is central throughout this entire diagnostic and therapeutic process. Physicians must help patients understand—as highlighted by Kim et al. [59]—that multifocal GGOs typically progress slowly and may be regarded as a 'chronic condition' requiring long-term management. Therapeutic goals center on controlling progression, eliminating the principal threat, and maintaining quality of life—not achieving complete radiological clearance. Plans must be tailored to patient age, pulmonary function, psychological status, and personal preferences, emphasizing the importance of dynamic surveillance [21]. Even postoperatively, lifelong monitoring of residual or newly emergent lesions is essential; surveillance strategies may incorporate risk-stratified management using the Lung Imaging Reporting and Data System (Lung-RADS) classification and clinical tumor probability models [1,3].

Recommendation 8: Management of multifocal GGOs should be individualized based on multidisciplinary team (MDT) discussion, integrating lesion radiological features, dynamic evolution, histopathological type, and patient-specific factors (2A); the MDT must include thoracic surgeons, pulmonologists, oncologists, radiation oncologists, radiologists, and pathologists. The dominant lesion should undergo sublobar resection (segmentectomy or wedge resection selected per size, CTR, and location); lobectomy is indicated when necessary—e.g., excessive lesion size, elevated CTR, inadequate sublobar margins, or adverse intraoperative FS findings such as high-risk histopathological subtypes or spread through air spaces (STAS) (2A). Secondary lesions should be managed primarily via regular surveillance (2A). Synchronous surgery requires rigorous assessment of pulmonary function and procedural trauma, strictly avoiding synchronous bilateral lobectomy (2A). Lymph node management should be guided by the dominant lesion’s imaging features and intraoperative FS findings—not routine systematic dissection (2B). For inoperable lesions, SBRT or ablation may be considered; among ablation modalities, thermal ablation demonstrates particular advantages for multifocal GGOs (2B). All decisions must be jointly formulated by physicians and patients, highlighting core objectives of long-term disease management, pulmonary functional preservation, and quality-of-life maintenance (2A).

3.4 Role of Non-Surgical Alternative Therapies

In GGO management, non-surgical alternatives—including SBRT and ablation—occupy a clearly defined, specific niche [15]. SBRT delivers ablative high-dose radiation from multiple angles with high spatial precision (typically completed in 4–8 fractions) [61], leveraging rapid dose falloff to spare adjacent normal tissues. It is non-invasive, avoids risks of bleeding or pneumothorax, and achieves high local control rates (5-year local control rate of 93% in inoperable patients) [62]; however, long-term risks of secondary malignancy—especially in adolescents—warrant caution [63]. Ablation is a minimally invasive procedure, primarily comprising radiofrequency ablation (RFA), microwave ablation (MWA), and cryoablation (CA), wherein percutaneous thermal or cryogenic energy directly destroys tumor tissue. It effectively preserves pulmonary function and permits repeat interventions—offering particular advantages for multifocal GGOs—yet carries puncture-related risks including pneumothorax (incidence 10–60%) and hemorrhage [60,64]. Both modalities inherently preclude acquisition of adequate pathological specimens for definitive diagnosis and molecular profiling—a fundamental limitation [65].

SBRT has become the standard alternative curative modality for early-stage peripheral NSCLC patients who are medically inoperable (e.g., poor cardiopulmonary function, advanced age, multiple comorbidities) or refuse surgery [15]. Strict adherence to indications is mandatory (typically T1–T2N0M0, ≤5 cm in diameter); central lesions (within 2 cm of the proximal bronchial tree) require special caution, employing more conservative fractionation schemes (e.g., 5–9 Gy per fraction) to mitigate severe risks such as bronchial fistula or massive hemorrhage. Ablation techniques differ: MWA delivers high energy and rapid heating, better overcoming the 'heat sink effect', making it suitable for larger lesions or those near vessels; CA provides superior analgesia and may stimulate antitumor immune responses, rendering it ideal for pleural-adjacent lesions; RFA remains the most established modality. Successful ablation hinges on achieving an adequate ablation margin—requiring ≥5–10 mm beyond the GGO tumor edge on post-ablation imaging [60,66].

For multifocal GGOs, both SBRT and ablation hold unique value in managing contralateral or ipsilateral secondary lesions and avoiding repeated surgical trauma. However, for patients physically fit for surgery, high-level evidence—such as large-scale phase III randomized controlled trials (RCTs)—demonstrating equivalence in long-term survival between non-surgical therapies and surgery remains lacking; thus, surgery remains the gold-standard first-line treatment [15]. Although thermal ablation may serve as a bridging or alternative strategy—e.g., delaying surgery or preserving pulmonary function—in select scenarios (e.g., intralobar lesions, definitively progressive lesions unsuitable for immediate lobectomy), its clinical application remains contentious, and domestic and international guidelines strictly limit its use as a first-line option [60]. While studies such as ROSEL/STARS [67] report encouraging SBRT outcomes, their evidence level is limited; hence, SBRT or ablation in this population should be considered exploratory. Selection between SBRT and ablation requires comprehensive MDT evaluation: central or larger lesions favor SBRT; peripheral or smaller lesions (particularly ≤3 cm) may be amenable to ablation—especially for patients desiring single-session treatment, cost sensitivity, or needing management of multiple dispersed lesions—while local technical capabilities and operator experience must be thoroughly considered [68].

Clinical decision-making for centrally located, progressive intralobar lesions poses particular difficulty [59]. Traditional strategies include continued surveillance until lobectomy criteria are met, or initial ablation of the progressive lesion followed by surgical resection upon recurrence. Although the latter may preserve pulmonary function for some patients, long-term efficacy data are lacking, mandating cautious application [60]. At this juncture, SDM assumes heightened importance: lesion characteristics, progression velocity, baseline pulmonary function, and patient preferences must be comprehensively weighed to balance the benefits and risks of ablation, surgery, and active surveillance.

SDM is central throughout this decision process. Physicians must comprehensively and objectively inform patients about the advantages and disadvantages of surgery, SBRT, ablation, and active surveillance—including surgery’s curative advantage versus its trauma risk; non-surgical modalities’ minimally invasive nature versus inherent uncertainties (e.g., inability to obtain adequate pathology, limited long-term data, controversy surrounding SBRT for operable patients, and puncture-related risks and unique efficacy assessment requirements for ablation)—to empower patients to make rational choices aligned with their clinical status, values, and quality-of-life priorities [62]. Patients in borderline states or facing multiple therapeutic options especially require thorough communication to clarify treatment goals and expectations, enabling collaborative development of individualized plans. Long-term, regular follow-up is mandatory post-treatment, utilizing contrast-enhanced CT (noting characteristic imaging evolutions post-ablation such as the 'fried-egg sign' or 'eggshell sign') or PET/CT (recommended at 6 months post-SBRT) to promptly assess treatment response and monitor for recurrence or new lesions [60].

Recommendation 9: SBRT and ablation serve as non-surgical alternatives for GGOs, primarily indicated for early-stage lung cancer patients who are medically inoperable or refuse surgery, and for secondary lesions in multifocal GGOs (with surgical resection remaining first-line for dominant lesions) (2A). All decisions must involve joint physician–patient participation, ensuring informed consent while balancing therapeutic efficacy and quality of life; long-term, regular follow-up is mandatory post-treatment (2A).

3.5 Reliability of Intraoperative Frozen Section (FS) Pathology

FS pathology is pivotal to GGO surgical decision-making and constitutes a critical consultation point between thoracic surgery and pathology departments [69]. Its value lies in providing rapid assessment of resected specimens within ~30 minutes, enabling real-time guidance for the surgeon on lesion characterization, resection extent, and lymph node management [70]. However, diagnostic reliability faces inherent limitations, primarily reflected in the discordance rate between intraoperative FS and final paraffin-embedded pathology. Reported concordance rates globally exceed 95%; yet, 'diagnostic upgrading'—where FS diagnoses AIS or MIA but final pathology confirms invasive adenocarcinoma (IAC)—is particularly noteworthy, occurring in 5–15% of cases [71].

This discrepancy arises from three interrelated factors: First, sampling error—pathologists must visually identify the 'most suspicious' region of the specimen within minutes, posing significant challenges for precise localization of infiltrative foci in small, morphologically irregular GGOs. Second, technical limitations of frozen section preparation—ice crystal artifacts and tissue wrinkling (more prevalent in soft lung tissue) degrade slide quality (graded as Class A, B, or C), directly compromising diagnostic clarity. Third, diagnostic interpretive difficulty—under time pressure, distinguishing subtle morphological differences among atypical adenomatous hyperplasia (AAH), AIS, MIA, and lepidic-predominant IAC introduces subjectivity and 'gray zones' [69]. Notably, a study [72] demonstrated that even with ~6.3% intraoperative underestimation (upgrading to IAC) in GGOs <2 cm with CTR <0.50, lymph node metastasis rates remain extremely low and 5-year RFS reaches 100%—indicating that such diagnostic upgrading typically does not compromise favorable prognosis [72].

This uncertainty fuels clinical decision-making controversies: one camp advocates full reliance on FS results, recommending sublobar resection without lymph node dissection for lesions meeting all of the following criteria—predominantly GGO appearance on imaging, diameter ≤ 2 cm, and intraoperative FS diagnosis of AIS or MIA—on the grounds that long-term oncologic outcomes after sublobar resection remain excellent even if upstaging occurs postoperatively, and uncertainty can be effectively managed via close surveillance; the other camp adopts a more cautious stance, recommending anatomical segmentectomy (to ensure adequate margins) or at least lymph node sampling—even when FS results are favorable [6,7]. Current consensus leans toward the former approach, particularly supported by high-level evidence such as the JCOG0804 trial; restrictive resection and limited lymph node management for GGO lesions meeting specific criteria have gained broad acceptance [31].

Whether secondary surgical lymph node dissection is warranted upon upstaging to invasive adenocarcinoma (IAC) on final paraffin-embedded pathology remains controversial [40]. The prevailing view [37] holds that for patients who underwent no initial lymph node dissection and whose final pathology reveals lepidic-predominant IAC with no clinical or radiologic evidence of lymph node metastasis, close surveillance—not prophylactic reoperation—is the preferred strategy, because the morbidity of secondary surgery likely outweighs its potential benefit.

Multiple strategies can enhance FS reliability: thorough intraoperative sampling of suspicious areas; real-time interdisciplinary communication between surgeons and pathologists (especially integrating preoperative CT features such as CTR into interpretation); and complementary use of intraoperative cytology imprinting. Shared decision-making (SDM) occupies a central role throughout this diagnostic and therapeutic process. Preoperatively, clinicians must comprehensively inform patients about both the utility and limitations of FS, explicitly disclosing the possibility of diagnostic upgrading and its generally favorable prognostic implications, and jointly deliberate contingency plans for various scenarios. Patients must understand the critical importance of surveillance and actively participate in formulating individualized surgical plans, striking an optimal balance between minimally invasive approaches and oncologic safety.

Recommendation 10: Frozen section (FS) pathology should serve as an important reference—not the sole determinant—in surgical decision-making for GGOs (Level 2A). When FS indicates AIS or MIA, sublobar resection without lymph node dissection constitutes a reasonable standard approach (Level 1B); when final paraffin-embedded pathology upgrades the diagnosis to lepidic-predominant IAC and there is no clinical or radiologic evidence of lymph node metastasis, close surveillance—not secondary surgery—is the preferred management (Level 2B). All decisions must integrate preoperative high-resolution CT characteristics, intraoperative findings, and pathological results. Preoperatively, clinicians must thoroughly discuss with patients the inherent uncertainties of frozen diagnosis and the spectrum of possible prognostic outcomes, collaboratively developing the surgical plan and postoperative surveillance strategy (Level 2A).

4 Postoperative Rehabilitation

4.1 Individualized Formulation of the Enhanced Recovery After Surgery (ERAS) Protocol

Individualized application of the ERAS philosophy during the perioperative period for GGOs represents a critical pathway to enhancing surgical safety and quality of recovery [73]. Its core principle is to tailor the ERAS protocol specifically to the biological characteristics of GGOs—namely, their indolent pathology, low lymph node metastasis rate, and frequent use of sublobar resection to preserve pulmonary function—thereby optimizing oncologic efficacy while minimizing surgical trauma, mitigating physiological stress, and accelerating functional recovery [74]. Given the high proportion of early-stage lung cancer among GGO patients, the increasing prevalence of elderly individuals with comorbidities, and the dominance of minimally invasive techniques (video-assisted thoracoscopic surgery [VATS] or robotic-assisted thoracoscopic surgery [RATS]), comprehensive implementation of ERAS has been shown to reduce hospital length of stay by 30–50% and decrease complication rates [53].

Formulation of an individualized ERAS protocol requires integration of multiple factors [74]. At the lesion level, small peripheral GGOs with intraoperative FS diagnosis of AIS or MIA permit aggressive minimally invasive strategies: priority use of uniportal VATS (U-VATS), aiming for 'tubeless' surgery (omitting chest tube placement entirely or using only a fine-bore catheter) or early chest tube removal (<450 mL/24 h drainage), and avoiding routine urinary catheterization—thus maximizing organ-sparing and expediting recovery. In contrast, for invasive carcinomas requiring lobectomy or systematic lymph node dissection, optimization focuses on balancing complete oncologic resection with refined analgesia and recovery support. Patient-related factors are equally pivotal: comprehensive preoperative cardiopulmonary assessment—including predicted postoperative forced expiratory volume in one second (ppoFEV1), predicted postoperative diffusing capacity of the lung for carbon monoxide (ppoDLCO), and cardiopulmonary exercise testing for maximal oxygen uptake (VO2max)—is essential. Younger, fitter patients may qualify for same-day discharge and intensive rehabilitation protocols; whereas elderly patients, those with multiple comorbidities, or high-risk individuals (e.g., smoking history ≥400 pack-years, borderline pulmonary function) require emphasis on prehabilitation (nutritional support, smoking cessation for ≥4 weeks, physical rehabilitation training) and cautious, safety-prioritized adjustment of ERAS components [75]. Recent prospective randomized controlled trials [76] support risk-stratified, multilevel individualized ERAS implementation—categorizing rehabilitation intensity according to surgical complexity, baseline patient status, and comorbidity burden—to maximize recovery benefits within a safe framework.

Anesthesia and intraoperative management directly influence ERAS effectiveness. Lung-protective ventilation is recommended, combined with multimodal analgesia integrating regional anesthesia (e.g., thoracic paravertebral block) and general anesthesia [routinely including acetaminophen and nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs)] to minimize opioid dependence [77]; goal-directed fluid management (preferably with balanced crystalloids, avoiding excessive infusion) [78]; and active warming plus multimodal antiemetic strategies to prevent postoperative nausea and vomiting [79]. Collectively, these measures attenuate surgical stress and lay the foundation for early mobilization and oral intake.

Postoperative management centers on core ERAS elements [80]: (1) Precise pain control—leveraging regional analgesia to mitigate opioid-induced gastrointestinal dysfunction, supplemented by physical therapies to accelerate recovery; (2) Early mobilization—encouraging ambulation within 24 hours postoperatively and prompt resumption of oral intake (clear liquids permitted up to 2 hours preoperatively; oral feeding initiated on the day of surgery); (3) Intensified pulmonary rehabilitation—including incentive spirometry, cycle ergometry, or stair climbing—to improve respiratory function and exercise tolerance [80,81]. Given the exceptional prognosis of GGO patients (e.g., 5-year recurrence-free survival [RFS] approaching 100% for AIS/MIA), postoperative surveillance may be appropriately streamlined (e.g., annual follow-up for the first two years), omitting routine brain MRI, bone scintigraphy, or PET/CT; remote healthcare delivery aligns well with the 'surgery-in-hospital, recovery-at-home' ERAS paradigm [82].

SDM across the entire perioperative continuum is pivotal to individualized ERAS implementation. Clinicians must thoroughly communicate lesion-specific characteristics, proposed surgical procedures, and corresponding rehabilitation pathways, helping patients grasp key concepts such as GGO indolence, the 'pre-metastatic phase', and the 'curative window'. Reasonable expectations regarding recovery should be co-established. Patient preferences regarding pain control, speed of recovery, and quality-of-life goals must be respected, and concrete measures—including prehabilitation, intraoperative anesthesia and surgical technique selection, postoperative analgesia, and early mobilization—must be jointly deliberated. Successful ERAS execution necessitates multidisciplinary team (MDT) collaboration: thoracic surgeons, anesthesiologists, nutritionists, rehabilitation specialists, and nursing staff must jointly formulate and implement an optimized, end-to-end protocol. Through continuous communication and dynamic reassessment, the rehabilitation trajectory is refined to enhance treatment adherence and overall satisfaction.

Recommendation 11: The ERAS philosophy should be comprehensively integrated into perioperative management for GGOs, with individualized optimization guided by lesion characteristics (pathological stage, CTR), surgical approach (sublobar vs. lobectomy), and patient factors (age, pulmonary function, comorbidities). For early-stage GGOs, minimally invasive surgery, precise lymph node management, aggressive tube management (early chest tube removal or tubeless surgery), and intensified postoperative pulmonary rehabilitation are strongly recommended; for complex surgeries or high-risk patients, ERAS components must be adjusted to prioritize safety, emphasizing prehabilitation. All ERAS protocols must be formulated through SDM and executed under MDT coordination, ensuring full patient understanding and engagement in rehabilitation to achieve reduced surgical trauma, lower complication rates, shortened hospital stays, and improved therapeutic experience (Level 2A).

4.2 Necessity and Stratification of Respiratory Rehabilitation

Respiratory rehabilitation holds a pivotal position in perioperative management of GGO patients and constitutes a cornerstone of the ERAS philosophy [83]. Its core value lies in systematically improving functional capacity and health education to effectively reduce the risk of postoperative pulmonary complications, enhance pulmonary function and exercise tolerance, improve quality of life, and alleviate psychological distress associated with both disease and surgery [84]. Due to substantial interindividual variability in age, baseline pulmonary function, exercise capacity, nutritional status, and surgical extent, respiratory rehabilitation protocols must be rigorously individualized. Scientific stratification of intensity based on preoperative assessment ensures the unification of safety and efficacy.

Based on preoperative assessment parameters—including age, degree of pulmonary function impairment, exercise tolerance, nutritional status, smoking history, and respiratory muscle strength—rehabilitation intensity can be stratified: for elderly patients, those with moderate-to-severe pulmonary impairment, low exercise endurance, or malnutrition, preoperative moderate-to-high-intensity rehabilitation training for 2–4 weeks is recommended to enhance surgical tolerance; for patients with near-normal or mildly impaired pulmonary function and good physical fitness, 1 week of prehabilitation suffices [75,85,86]. Exercise modalities should encompass aerobic training, resistance training, and respiratory muscle-specific training, integrated with airway clearance techniques, early mobilization, and health education with psychosocial support, forming a multidimensional intervention system [83,85,86]. Close monitoring of patient responses throughout is mandatory to avoid excessive fatigue and to identify contraindications—including fever, severe thrombocytopenia, or osseous metastases—to ensure rehabilitation safety.

SDM is crucial for both formulation and implementation of respiratory rehabilitation [87,88]. Clinicians must detail the significance, anticipated benefits, and potential risks of respiratory rehabilitation to patients, helping them recognize its positive impact on postoperative recovery and complication reduction [85], while simultaneously gaining thorough insight into patients’ physical capacity, personal preferences, and practical feasibility to jointly establish realistic rehabilitation goals and plans. For instance, younger patients desiring rapid return to daily activities may tolerate slightly higher exercise intensities; conversely, elderly patients with multiple comorbidities and poor tolerance should follow a safety-first, stepwise progression plan [89]. Continuous communication and dynamic adjustment foster trust-based clinician–patient partnerships, thereby enhancing rehabilitation adherence and overall effectiveness.

Recommendation 12: All GGO patients undergoing surgery should receive individualized, stratified respiratory rehabilitation interventions. Preoperatively, rehabilitation needs should be assessed based on age, pulmonary function, exercise tolerance, and nutritional status, followed by 1–4 weeks of moderate-to-high-intensity prehabilitation. Early postoperative mobilization should be encouraged, combined with aerobic and resistance training alongside respiratory technique instruction to promote functional recovery. Rehabilitation protocols must be co-developed through clinician–patient discussion, balancing scientific validity, safety, and practicality; ongoing communication and adaptive adjustments during implementation will maximize perioperative experience and long-term quality of life (Level 2A).

4.3 Postoperative Pain Management Strategies

Postoperative pain management for GGOs is a critical component of the ERAS pathway. The strategy has evolved from drug-dependent or single-modality analgesia to individualized, multimodal analgesia anchored by ultrasound-guided regional blocks and complemented by diverse analgesic agents [53,77]. Effective pain control fulfills both the demand for comfort-centered care and serves as a key enabler for restoring cardiorespiratory and neurological functions and accelerating recovery. Comprehensive pain management significantly reduces the incidence of postoperative atelectasis, pulmonary infection, and mortality. The core protocol emphasizes MDT collaboration and individualized, multimodal intervention throughout the perioperative period: preoperative assessment of pain sensitivity, preemptive analgesia with acetaminophen plus NSAIDs to elevate pain thresholds; intraoperative regional blockade to interrupt nociceptive signaling at its source; and postoperative scheduled non-opioid analgesics as the foundation, augmented by intravenous patient-controlled analgesia (PCA) pumps and rescue doses of opioids as needed [77,90].

Regional blockade should prioritize ultrasound-guided precision techniques covering the surgical field, primarily including thoracic paravertebral block (TPVB), erector spinae plane block (ESPB), serratus anterior plane block, and intercostal nerve block—all demonstrating robust analgesic efficacy. Although thoracic epidural analgesia remains the gold standard for regional pain control, its technical complexity and higher complication risk have led to its gradual replacement by precise peripheral nerve blocks. Ultrasound-guided regional blocks deliver reliable analgesia, with TPVB exhibiting particularly outstanding efficacy; serratus anterior plane block, ESPB, and intercostal nerve block demonstrate non-inferior efficacy compared to the gold standard. Serratus anterior plane block offers simple needle positioning, applicability in supine or lateral decubitus positions, and high safety due to rib protection of the needle tip—making it suitable for rescue analgesia in complex pain scenarios [91]. Opioid utilization is trending toward 'opioid-sparing': sufficient regional blockade and background non-opioid analgesia reduce opioid requirements, thereby lowering the incidence of adverse effects such as nausea, vomiting, and respiratory depression [88].

Individualization is another cornerstone of high-quality analgesia [77]. In elderly patients, potential hepatic or renal impairment necessitates dose adjustment and vigilant monitoring for respiratory depression; patients with high pain sensitivity require preoperative planning of intensified multimodal analgesia, possibly involving dedicated pain management consultation; for 'tubeless' procedures (e.g., spontaneous breathing anesthesia), enhanced regional blockade is essential to avoid compromising respiratory function. Pain assessment must address both resting and dynamic pain, encouraging active patient participation in evaluation and enabling real-time protocol adjustments based on feedback [92].

SDM is indispensable throughout the pain management process. The pain management team must preoperatively inform patients about the inevitability and controllability of postoperative pain, describe the planned analgesic strategy and expected sensations (e.g., chest numbness following regional block), help establish realistic expectations, and clarify that the primary pain control objective is to facilitate early mobilization and effective coughing—not absolute pain elimination. Clinicians and patients may jointly define an acceptable pain threshold—for example, a numeric rating scale (NRS) score ≤3 [88]—and co-formulate a post-discharge analgesia plan centered on non-opioid medications. Continuous communication and dynamic adjustment enhance patient engagement in rehabilitation, treatment adherence, and overall satisfaction [77].

Recommendation 13: Postoperative pain management for GGOs should adopt a multimodal analgesia protocol centered on ultrasound-guided regional blocks (e.g., TPVB) combined with acetaminophen, NSAIDs, and other analgesics, while restricting opioid use. Analgesic strategies must be individualized according to patient age, hepatic/renal function, surgical approach, and pain history, implemented collaboratively by an MDT pain management team, and co-determined with patients regarding pain control targets and rehabilitation plans to achieve safe, comfortable, and rapid recovery (Level 2A).

5 Postoperative Adjuvant Therapy

Decision-making regarding postoperative adjuvant therapy for ground-glass opacity (GGO) lung adenocarcinoma represents a clinical challenge. The central controversy lies in precisely identifying the patient subgroup that truly benefits from adjuvant therapy while avoiding overtreatment of indolent lesions. Unlike conventional solid non-small cell lung cancer, GGO-predominant lung adenocarcinoma exhibits excellent prognosis and low recurrence risk; thus, adjuvant therapy must be applied with caution [4]. After complete resection, adenocarcinoma in situ (AIS) and minimally invasive adenocarcinoma (MIA) carry virtually no recurrence risk; accordingly, the consensus explicitly states that adjuvant therapy is unnecessary [4,9,93]. For invasive adenocarcinoma (IAC), individualized assessment integrating pathological stage, high-risk features, and molecular characteristics is required. Patients with stage IA disease without high-risk pathological factors—such as micropapillary/solid components, lymphovascular invasion, or spread through air spaces (STAS)—typically do not warrant adjuvant therapy. In contrast, for stage IA patients harboring high-risk pathological features (e.g., ≥10% solid/micropapillary/complex glandular components) and epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR) mutations, the prospective APPOINT study by Zhang et al. [94] demonstrated that postoperative adjuvant treatment with the third-generation EGFR-tyrosine kinase inhibitor (TKI) aumolertinib significantly improved 2-year recurrence-free survival (RFS) from 75% to 100%, with favorable safety, thereby filling an unmet therapeutic need and providing high-level evidence for personalized adjuvant therapy in this population. Decision-making for stage IB disease remains contentious due to variable recurrence risk and necessitates comprehensive evaluation of tumor size, differentiation grade, and pleural invasion. Patients with stage II or higher disease frequently require adjuvant therapy, with regimen selection guided by nodal status and driver gene mutation profiles [93,95].

Current deep-seated controversies include: (1) the limited applicability of the conventional tumor-node-metastasis (TNM) staging system to GGO-predominant lung adenocarcinoma; (2) the potential clinical value and implementation challenges of minimal residual disease (MRD) detection [96]; and (3) the benefit–risk ratio of adjuvant targeted therapy in patients with driver gene–positive tumors (e.g., EGFR mutations). Particularly for early-stage IAC—even when sensitive driver mutations are present—clinicians must carefully weigh the inherently low recurrence risk against the adverse effects and economic burden associated with targeted therapy [36,97]. Furthermore, multiple GGOs are often multifocal primary lesions, potentially harboring heterogeneous driver gene alterations across different nodules; therefore, adjuvant therapy decisions should be based on the highest-stage lesion rather than applied uniformly to all GGOs [8,41].

This decision-making process is inherently uncertain, rendering shared decision-making (SDM) critically important [98]. Clinicians must comprehensively assess individual recurrence risk—not only via pathological staging but also incorporating the consolidation-to-tumor ratio (CTR), histological subtype, surgical resection status, and molecular biomarkers—and clearly communicate to patients their personalized recurrence risk, the absolute clinical benefit of adjuvant therapy, potential adverse effects, and economic costs. Importantly, active surveillance is itself a valid and reasonable management option. For patients in borderline clinical scenarios—such as stage IB disease or stage IA disease with high-risk pathological features—thorough discussion is essential to understand their level of anxiety about recurrence, tolerance for treatment-related side effects, and personal values and preferences, enabling co-development of a plan aligned with both their goals and quality-of-life priorities. Multidisciplinary team (MDT) discussion serves as a vital support mechanism for complex cases, integrating expert opinions across disciplines to enhance the scientific rigor and comprehensiveness of decision-making [99,100].

Recommendation 14: Adjuvant therapy is not recommended after surgery for AIS or MIA (Level 1A); adjuvant therapy is not recommended for stage IA IAC patients lacking high-risk pathological features or molecular abnormalities (Level 1B); for stage IA IAC patients who underwent sublobar resection and harbor EGFR mutations with ≥10% solid/micropapillary/complex glandular components, EGFR-TKI–based adjuvant therapy may be cautiously considered following thorough MDT evaluation and SDM, provided that the potential benefits, adverse effects, and economic burden are fully disclosed to the patient (Level 2B); for stage IB patients, adjuvant therapy decisions require careful weighing of benefits versus risks, integrating assessment of high-risk factors and genomic profiling results (Level 2B); for stage II or higher patients, adjuvant chemotherapy, targeted therapy, or immunotherapy is recommended, selected according to nodal status and driver gene mutation profile (Level 1A). All such decisions must be grounded in individualized recurrence risk assessment, patient preferences, and quality-of-life considerations, formulated jointly by clinicians and patients and supported by MDT deliberation, to prevent both overtreatment and undertreatment (Level 2A). Centers with appropriate resources are encouraged to explore precision oncology technologies—including MRD detection—for refined recurrence risk stratification and optimization of adjuvant therapy strategies.

6 Postoperative Follow-up

6.1 Individualized Controversies Regarding Follow-up Frequency and Duration

Postoperative follow-up for GGO is a critical component of long-term management. Globally, no uniform standard exists for follow-up frequency or duration. The core controversy centers on tailoring follow-up regimens to individual recurrence risk, balancing effective recurrence surveillance against the harms of overmedicalization and psychological burden [9,82]. Disagreement regarding follow-up frequency primarily concerns fixed-interval schedules versus dynamic, risk-stratified adjustment [101]. Traditional guidelines commonly recommend biannual imaging during the first two postoperative years, followed by annual surveillance thereafter; however, this approach fails to account for the marked heterogeneity among GGO patients [82]. Increasingly, a risk-stratified model is advocated: high-risk patients—including those with IAC exhibiting high-risk histological subtypes, lymphovascular invasion, STAS, or nodal positivity—are advised to undergo intensive surveillance every 3–6 months during early follow-up [102], whereas low-risk patients—such as those with AIS, MIA, or lepidic-predominant stage I cancers without high-risk features—exhibit extremely low recurrence risk and may safely transition to annual surveillance or even longer intervals [5,36].

Controversy surrounding follow-up duration centers on lifelong surveillance versus time-limited surveillance [82]. Proponents of lifelong follow-up argue that lung cancer survivors remain at persistent risk for second primary cancers, and given the indolent biology of some GGOs, late recurrences may occur beyond 5 or even 10 years, necessitating extended monitoring. Opponents contend that for patients with AIS, MIA, or select low-risk stage I cancers, the probability of recurrence beyond 5 years is exceedingly low; thus, prolonged routine radiographic surveillance yields poor cost-effectiveness and imposes unnecessary radiation exposure and psychological stress, supporting termination of scheduled imaging surveillance after 5–10 years [36]. Additionally, disagreement persists regarding optimal imaging modalities: low-dose computed tomography (LDCT) is favored for long-term screening due to its low radiation dose, yet some experts recommend baseline and early follow-up standard-dose CT for high-risk patients to obtain higher-resolution images facilitating precise longitudinal comparison [1,82].

An individualized follow-up strategy must integrate pathological subtype, surgical details, high-risk features, and patient age [102]. For very-low-risk patients (AIS/MIA), annual surveillance suffices, with interval extension or discontinuation negotiable after 5 years [36]; low-risk stage I patients should undergo surveillance for at least 5 years, with subsequent interval relaxation considered on a case-by-case basis; intermediate- to high-risk patients require long-term, possibly lifelong, regular surveillance [102]. For patients with multiple GGOs, follow-up planning should be anchored to the highest-risk resected lesion, while concurrently maintaining long-term surveillance of untreated secondary nodules [8,59]. Younger low-risk patients remain candidates for long-term follow-up, whereas older patients or those with multiple comorbidities may undergo simplified follow-up protocols following thorough clinician–patient discussion [102].

SDM occupies a central role throughout postoperative follow-up. Clinicians must thoroughly explain to patients the dual objectives of surveillance—recurrence monitoring and detection of second primary cancers—as well as the rationale underpinning the individualized follow-up plan, including pathological findings, surgical outcomes, and stratified recurrence risk levels [101], enabling joint evaluation of the advantages and disadvantages of alternative strategies. Intensive surveillance enables earlier detection of abnormalities but increases financial and psychological burdens; conversely, less frequent surveillance alleviates these pressures but may marginally elevate the risk of interval recurrence—though this risk remains extremely low for low-risk patients [101]. Simultaneously, clinicians must respect patients’ subjective risk perception and psychological resilience, flexibly adjusting follow-up plans within medical principles and preserving the capacity for dynamic optimization based on serial imaging results [101].

Recommendation 15: Risk-stratified, individualized follow-up is recommended after GGO surgery. For low-risk patients (AIS, MIA, and lepidic-predominant stage I cancers without high-risk features), surveillance should occur every 6–12 months during the first 1–2 postoperative years, then annually thereafter; after 5 years, intervals may be extended at discretion. For intermediate- to high-risk patients (stage I cancers with high-risk pathological features, or stage II and higher), surveillance should occur every 3–6 months during the first 2 years, then every 6–12 months thereafter, with long-term adherence maintained. Low-dose CT (LDCT) is the primary modality for all patients; for high-risk patients, baseline and early follow-up standard-dose CT may be considered. All follow-up plans must be co-developed through thorough clinician–patient communication, dynamically adjusted to reflect clinical characteristics, psychological needs, and patient preferences, thereby achieving an optimal balance between scientifically sound surveillance and reasonable burden (Level 2A).

6.2 Evidence-Based Optimization of Follow-up Imaging Modalities

Optimizing postoperative follow-up imaging modalities for GGO is a clinically significant issue, centered on implementing differential surveillance strategies tailored to individual recurrence risk—balancing timely recurrence detection against the burden of excessive medical interventions [9,102]. This consensus advocates transitioning from traditional ‘one-size-fits-all’ approaches toward individualized follow-up regimens informed by pathological subtype, stage, and high-risk features. Given their excellent prognosis and negligible recurrence risk, AIS and MIA patients require only annual thin-section chest CT for surveillance; routine head MRI, whole-body bone scintigraphy, or PET/CT yield extremely low diagnostic yield while unnecessarily increasing radiation exposure, economic cost, and psychological distress [4]. For IAC, further risk stratification is warranted: low-risk patients (e.g., stage IA without high-risk features) should undergo chest CT every 6 months during the first 2 postoperative years, transitioning to annual surveillance thereafter; high-risk patients (e.g., stage IB or higher, or those with lymphovascular invasion, STAS, or solid/micropapillary components) require more intensive surveillance, including periodic chest CT plus selective use of tumor markers, brain MRI, or abdominal imaging—avoiding indiscriminate comprehensive screening [5,103].

When selecting specific follow-up modalities, clinicians must rigorously evaluate the indications and evidentiary support for each test [9]. PET/CT demonstrates limited sensitivity for GGO-predominant lesions due to their low metabolic activity and is therefore not recommended for routine postoperative surveillance [82]; tumor markers exhibit low positive predictive value and specificity in early-stage GGO patients and should serve only as adjunctive references for high-risk individuals [82]; bronchoscopy offers minimal utility for asymptomatic peripheral lesions and is indicated only for centrally located lesions or specific symptoms such as hemoptysis. Moreover, for patients with multiple GGOs, follow-up planning must be anchored to the pathological characteristics of the highest-risk resected nodule, while simultaneously maintaining long-term surveillance of untreated nodules [8,25]. Throughout follow-up, clinicians must balance the clinical significance of the ‘curative window period’ for low-risk patients against the physiological and psychological burdens of excessive testing, emphasizing precision and cost-effectiveness in surveillance design.

SDM occupies a central role. Clinicians must thoroughly explain to patients their individualized recurrence risk and the rationale underlying the proposed follow-up plan—including the justification for recommended tests and omitted modalities—to foster understanding of the delicate equilibrium between avoiding overtreatment and ensuring adequate safety [82]. They must also explicitly articulate the dual goals of surveillance (recurrence monitoring and second primary cancer detection) and educate patients to recognize warning symptoms—including new-onset cough, hemoptysis, bone pain, or headache—that warrant prompt medical evaluation irrespective of scheduled follow-up timing [82]. For patients with heightened anxiety or divergent risk perceptions, clinicians should respectfully accommodate their preferences within the bounds of evidence-based medicine, adjusting follow-up frequency or modalities as needed to improve adherence and quality of life [13]. Follow-up plans must remain dynamic and flexible, with subsequent strategies promptly revised based on each imaging result [101].

Recommendation 16: Risk-stratified management of follow-up imaging modalities is recommended after GGO surgery: AIS and MIA patients require only annual chest LDCT; low-risk stage I IAC patients should undergo chest CT every 6 months for the first 2 years, then annually thereafter; high-risk patients (stage IB or higher, or those with high-risk features) require more intensive surveillance, including periodic chest CT plus selective use of tumor markers, brain MRI, or abdominal imaging—but routine PET/CT, bronchoscopy, or whole-body bone scintigraphy should be avoided. All follow-up strategies must be co-developed through clinician–patient communication, dynamically adjusted to reflect pathological characteristics, psychological needs, and patient preferences, thereby unifying scientifically rigorous surveillance with reasonable burden (Level 2A).

6.3 Differentiation and Management Strategies for New Lesions Detected During Follow-up

Differentiating and managing newly detected lesions during GGO follow-up presents a common clinical challenge, requiring integrated assessment of imaging features, dynamic evolution, and individual patient factors [101]. Such lesions may represent benign inflammation, a second primary lung cancer, or metastatic recurrence. Key discriminators include nodule density, margin morphology, growth rate, and evolution of solid components [5,21,25]. For a newly identified pure GGO (pGGO) lacking overt malignant features, short-term surveillance (e.g., 2–3 months) or empirical anti-inflammatory therapy followed by repeat imaging is recommended to exclude transient inflammatory changes. If the lesion persists but remains stable, long-term close monitoring is warranted; progression—defined as increase in diameter or growth of solid components—should trigger consideration of intervention [3,9,21,25]. For lesions highly suspicious for metastasis or recurrence, multidisciplinary team (MDT) discussion should be initiated to formulate an individualized systemic management plan—potentially encompassing surgery, targeted therapy, radiotherapy, or immunotherapy—based on comprehensive staging, molecular profiling, and the patient’s overall condition [101,104].

Decision-making must fully consider pulmonary functional reserve, age, comorbidities, and psychological status. Particularly for patients with prior pulmonary resection, repeat surgery requires meticulous assessment of pulmonary functional tolerance. Advanced technologies—including thin-section CT, artificial intelligence–assisted diagnosis, and radiomics—can enhance diagnostic precision. SDM is critically important: clinicians must explain to patients the spectrum of possible etiologies for new lesions, clarify the intent and safety of surveillance, and help them understand the rationale for conservative observation of indolent lesions within the ‘curative window period’, thereby preventing unwarranted panic and unnecessary interventions [3,13]. Through collaborative deliberation, clinicians and patients jointly weigh the pros and cons of alternative options, respecting the patient’s personal valuation of treatment risks and quality-of-life impact, ultimately formulating an evidence-based, patient-centered, individualized management strategy.

Recommendation 17: For newly detected lesions after GGO surgery, short-term surveillance is the preferred initial strategy to differentiate benign causes. Lesions that persist but remain stable warrant long-term radiographic monitoring. Progressive lesions may be considered for surgical resection—preferentially sublobar resection—or other local therapies. Suspected metastatic or recurrent lesions require formal MDT discussion to determine a systemic treatment strategy. All decisions must be grounded in lesion characteristics, the patient’s overall clinical status, and personal preferences, co-developed through thorough clinician–patient communication, ensuring patient safety while avoiding excessive diagnostic or therapeutic interventions (Level 2A).

7 Summary

GGO management has entered a new era of precision medicine centered on ‘patient values’ [2,6,7,10]. This consensus systematically addresses key controversies in GGO management and, for the first time, establishes SDM as the overarching principle guiding the entire care continuum [12,98]. SDM transcends traditional clinician–patient relationship models by emphasizing balanced integration of scientific evidence and individual preferences, transforming clinical uncertainty into opportunities for personalized decision-making through structured dialogue [98].

GGO exhibits pronounced biological heterogeneity and indolent behavior, rendering traditional ‘one-size-fits-all’ strategies overly simplistic. This consensus explicitly affirms that every phase—from initiation and interval determination of screening, to intraoperative surgical modality selection and postoperative rehabilitation pathways—must integrate evidence-based medicine with the patient’s life goals, risk tolerance, and fundamental life values [6,9,16,80]. SDM does not delegate decision-making solely to patients; rather, it fosters a collaborative partnership: clinicians provide expert interpretation of evidence and clinical judgment, while patients contribute contextualized needs and preference weights, culminating in a truly individualized care plan.

To enhance the practical implementation of SDM, the following pathways and tools are recommended: First, conduct structured clinician–patient dialogues at key decision points—such as timing and modality of surgery—systematically explaining concepts including GGO indolence, the ‘pre-metastatic success phase,’ and the ‘curative window period,’ performing individualized risk stratification based on imaging features, and clearly articulating the anticipated benefits, risks, and long-term quality-of-life implications of all options—including active surveillance, sublobar resection, lobectomy, and non-surgical local therapies. Second, develop and deploy decision-support tools, such as risk-visualization charts (Supplementary Table 1, http://www.lungca.org/files/2025s167s1.xlsx), comparative choice cards, and online progression-probability calculators, accompanied by patient education handbooks [105]; translate technical terms—including CTR and growth rate—into accessible metaphors and everyday language, avoiding direct presentation of raw data. Third, optimize communication processes by promoting techniques such as ‘ask–answer–explain,’ encouraging family participation, and establishing dedicated SDM consultation clinics to ensure adequate time for discussion. Fourth, leverage MDT support systems [106], with nurses or case managers assisting in patient education and exploring the role of decision coaches; hospital leadership should incorporate SDM training and practice-quality metrics into institutional healthcare quality evaluation frameworks, thereby constructing a comprehensive, actionable framework spanning content, tools, communication, and systemic support.

This consensus aims to strengthen collaborative, patient-value-oriented diagnostic and therapeutic approaches across thoracic surgery, respiratory medicine, and oncology in the management of GGOs. It is anticipated that implementing a structured communication and decision-making process centered on shared decision-making (SDM) will enhance healthcare quality and patient satisfaction, reduce unnecessary examinations and treatments—and associated resource waste—and foster harmonious physician–patient relationships.
