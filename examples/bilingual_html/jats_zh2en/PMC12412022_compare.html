源（中文）

当前仪器分析课程教学存在着两个相互交织的困境［1］：其一，单向知识传输模式主导课堂，使学生陷入被动接受式的学习范式，严重制约了学习者主体性的发挥，导致高阶思维能力培养目标达成度偏低；其二，现代信息技术与课程体系的有机整合滞后，数字化教学资源开发不足。色谱分析是仪器分析课程的重要组成部分，其教学过程也存在上述问题。近年来，交互式模拟工具的出现为解决这些问题带来了新希望，能显著提升学生对抽象科学概念的理解［2，3］，但色谱法的交互式教学工具仍相对匮乏，尤其在兼顾理论深度与初学者友好性方面存在明显缺口。

已有研究报道了面向高阶学习者（包括专业化学从业人员）的色谱分离优化模拟软件［4‒6］，但这些软件因复杂性、操作专业性以及与学习者认知水平匹配度不足等原因，还难以适配本科生色谱分析入门课程的教学需求。Jordheim等［7］使用JavaScript编写代码开发了基于交互式网页的色谱教学工具，服务于塔板理论与基础色谱原理入门学习，但它发布在Github平台，不方便在我们国内课堂教学中使用。

在色谱分析的教学体系中，塔板理论常安排在教材和课堂讲授靠前位置。该理论由色谱技术的先驱Martin与Synge提出，已成为色谱界广泛接受的理论。尽管某些动力学模型（如速率理论）在理论上更为精准，但塔板理论仍然是色谱学教育的基石。GeoGebra作为一款优异的云端计算平台，在教学中发挥着越来越重要的作用。在总结了2010‒2020年有关GeoGebra教学应用期刊论文基础上，Yohannes等［8］得出这样的结论：利用GeoGebra的可视化功能可降低学习认知负荷。Kostic等［9］的工作证实，使用GeoGebra能有效提升学生解决溶液定量组成问题的学习成效。Lossjew等［10］将数字工具的基本教学功能与化学研究过程相结合，利用GeoGebra辅助探索了化学反应进程。冷却曲线及其数学模型为相变过程中的潜热、热传递、吉布斯相规则和质量传递动力学提供了丰富的信息。Shi等［11］通过将冷却曲线模型与GeoGebra绘图相结合的教学方法，加深了高年级本科生对复杂相变过程的理解，并有效增强了学生的数学建模、计算和解决问题的能力。我们［12，13］曾介绍过GeoGebra云端计算平台开展化学分析教学的应用案例。本文以塔板理论教学为切入点，以GeoGebra云端计算平台为教学环境，创建了相互衔接、渐次升级的3个学生活动模块（单组分色谱流出曲线模拟、多组分色谱流出曲线模拟和色谱保留时间方程），形成了“理论模型-问题案例-数字工具-可视化模拟”有机结合的教学链和学习链，提高了学生课堂教学中的参与度。本文所开发的塔板理论色谱图模拟过程具有如下突出优点：（1）工具开源和免费；（2）适用于手机、电脑等多终端，可在课堂教学中便捷运用；（3）可支持学生调节参数自主学习（如化合物数量、流动相流速、死时间、相比和塔板数等），增加学生课堂参与度和获得感。

1 理论模型的整合与重构

在大多数仪器分析课程的色谱教学体系中，教材内容和授课安排通常以塔板理论作为教学起点。教材中普遍会给出塔板理论数学模型，但较少将描述色谱过程的其他参数（如体积流速、死体积、死时间、相比和保留时间等）引入。本文将上述参数引入到塔板理论数学模型中建立了以流动相运行时间为自变量的综合模型，为构建交互式塔板理论教学工具奠定了化学基础。

1.1 塔板理论常见模型

若色谱柱有nn块理论塔板，则色谱柱出口处流动相中的溶质浓度由式（1）给出［7］。式（1）是塔板理论的泊松函数，当塔板数nn足够大时，非常接近于高斯分布函数。

C=11+KC01n!e−vvnC = \frac{1}{1 + K}C_{0}\frac{1}{n!}e^{- v}v^{n}

式中，CC和C0C_{0}分别是溶质在色谱柱出口的浓度及初始浓度，KK是分配系数。式（2）给出vv的定义［7］，它表示与折合塔板体积有关的物理量，单位是“塔板体积”而非mL。

v=VKvs+vmv = \frac{V}{Kv_{s} + v_{m}}

式中，VV是消耗的流动相体积，vsv_{s}和vmv_{m}分别是每块塔板中固定相和流动相的体积。

1.2 塔板理论模型的近似变换

由于式（1）中存在阶乘的计算，运算量较大，不便于后期的模拟，因此要进行近似处理。当n>10n > 10，阶乘运算可以按照Stirling关系式计算：

n!≈2πn(ne)nn! \approx \sqrt[{}]{2\pi n}{(\frac{n}{e})}^{n}

将式（3）代入式（1），则有：

C=11+KC012πn(vn)nen−vC = \frac{1}{1 + K}C_{0}\frac{1}{\sqrt[{}]{2\pi n}}{(\frac{v}{n})}^{n}e^{n - v}

1.3 塔板理论模型中时间变量的引入

经过上面的处理后，塔板理论模型的数学表达为式（4）所描述的动态方程及式（2）约束条件。若KK 、 vsv_{s}、vmv_{m}、nn和C0C_{0}均为定值时，则CC是vv的函数，此时只能得到CC-vv图，而不是通常所见的CC-tt色谱图，因此需要将时间变量引入到式（2）约束条件中。

流动相的消耗体积VV等于流动相的体积流速FF与运行时间tt的乘积：

V=F⋅tV = F \cdot t

同理，死体积VmV_{m}等于流动相的体积流速FF与死时间tmt_{m}的乘积：

Vm=F⋅tmV_{m} = F \cdot t_{m}

因为色谱柱由nn块塔板构成，所以死体积等于每块塔板中流动相体积vmv_{m}与nn的乘积：

Vm=n⋅vmV_{m} = n \cdot v_{m}

相比β\beta存在如下关系：

β=VmVs=nvmnvs=vmvs\beta = \frac{V_{m}}{V_{s}} = \frac{nv_{m}}{nv_{s}} = \frac{v_{m}}{v_{s}}

将式（5）~（8）代入到式（2），得：

v=VKvs+vm=Ft(K/β+1)vm=Ft(K/β+1)Vm/n=tn(K/β+1)tmv = \frac{V}{Kv_{s} + v_{m}} = \frac{Ft}{(K/\beta + 1)v_{m}} = \frac{Ft}{(K/\beta + 1)V_{m}/n} = \frac{tn}{(K/\beta + 1)t_{m}}

至此，整合后的塔板理论模型数学表达包括式（4）所描述的动态方程及时间变量参数引入后的式（9）约束条件。这样当KK 、 vsv_{s}、vmv_{m}、nn和C0C_{0}均为定值时，CC是tt的函数，理论上可以得到符合常规色谱图形状的CC-tt图。

2 理论模型的教学应用

2.1 软件平台的选择

为了使学生在色谱课堂学习时可以获得自己模拟的色谱图，教学软件的选择是重要的环节。为了保证教学活动的顺利开展以及学生正常学习，需要选择一款开源免费软件平台。GeoGebra云端计算平台不仅开源免费，可以在手机、平板电脑和台式电脑等多终端运行，而且编程过程接近自然语言，非常适合在课堂环境下使用［12，13］。在此基础上，我们构建了一种塔板理论教学新模式。该模式依托相互衔接、渐次升级的3个学生活动模块（单组分色谱流出曲线模拟、多组分色谱流出曲线模拟和色谱保留时间方程推导），形成了“理论模型-问题案例-数字工具-可视化模拟”的教学链和学习链，提高了学生在塔板理论学习过程中的课堂参与度，使学生在理论认知深度、数字化建模能力、问题解决思维等方面同步提升。

2.4 学生活动模块3——保留时间方程的推导

为了探究前述发现的保留时间和分配系数间的线性依存关系，解释式（10）中所蕴含的化学意义，引导学生重新审视前面的式（4）和式（9）色谱流出曲线模型。在所模拟的色谱流出曲线中最醒目的部分就是色谱峰，也就意味着式（4）中的浓度CC存在极大值，就是说应有dCdt=0\frac{dC}{dt} = 0。为了避免学生陷入长时间数学推导过程而影响本课程的进度，决定采用国产AI模型“豆包（版本7.8.0）”（下文简称“豆包”）辅助推导。

首先，将式（9）和式（4）放到一起，如下：

v(t)=tntm(1+K/β)v(t) = \frac{tn}{t_{m}(1 + K/\beta)}

f(t)=11+KC012πn(v(t)n)n⋅en−v(t)f(t) = \frac{1}{1 + K}C_{0}\frac{1}{\sqrt[{}]{2\pi n}}{(\frac{v(t)}{n})}^{n} \cdot e^{n - v(t)}

其次，将式（9）和式（4）拍成照片上传到“豆包”中，然后写一句“根据上图的两个式子，求f(t)f(t)导数等于零时的条件关系式”。“豆包”接到指令后就会给出以下3个详细步骤：分析f(t)f(t)的结构，利用乘积求导法则(uv)′=u′v+uv′{(uv)}^{'} = u^{'}v + uv^{'}求f(t)f(t)的导数，令f′(t)=0f^{'}(t) = 0求解条件关系式，最终得到：

t=tm(1+K/β)=tm(1+k)t = t_{m}(1 + K/\beta) = t_{m}(1 + k)

式中kk代表着保留因子或分配比。式（11）就是常说的保留时间方程，它揭示了影响色谱保留时间的本质。当色谱柱选定和流速确定后，也就意味着相比β\beta和死时间tmt_{m}是不变的量，所以式（11）表明保留时间tt就是分配系数KK的函数，并从本质上证明了分配系数越大则保留时间越长这一前述实验观察和猜测的正确性。由式（11）可推知式（10）的初始实验条件为tm=3t_{m} = 3和β=2\beta = 2，与表2所给初始条件一致。

目前教材中保留时间方程往往是直接给出的，逻辑上显得比较突兀，不利于学生创新思维的培养。本文从色谱流出曲线方程派生出保留时间方程这种做法，不仅拓宽了色谱流出曲线方程的应用范畴，锻炼了学生的逻辑思维能力和AI工具辅助学习的能力，而且增加了色谱课程的理论完整性。

Contemporary instrumental analysis instruction faces two interrelated pedagogical challenges [1]: First, unidirectional knowledge transmission dominates classroom instruction, confining students to passive reception and severely constraining the development of learner agency, thereby resulting in low achievement of higher-order thinking objectives; second, organic integration of modern information technology with the curriculum remains lagging, and the development of digital instructional resources is insufficient. Chromatographic analysis constitutes a core component of instrumental analysis courses, and its instruction suffers from these same issues. In recent years, the emergence of interactive simulation tools has offered new promise for resolving such challenges, significantly enhancing students’ comprehension of abstract scientific concepts [2, 3]; however, interactive teaching tools specifically tailored for chromatography remain relatively scarce, particularly those balancing theoretical depth with beginner-friendliness.

Jordheim et al. Within the chromatographic pedagogy framework, plate theory is conventionally positioned early in both textbooks and lecture sequences. Originally proposed by chromatographic pioneers Martin and Synge, this theory has become widely accepted across the chromatographic community. Although certain kinetic models (e.g., rate theory) offer greater theoretical precision, plate theory remains the cornerstone of chromatographic education. 1 Integration and Reconstruction of Theoretical Models

In most instrumental analysis curricula, chromatographic instruction typically commences with plate theory. While standard textbooks commonly present the mathematical model of plate theory, they rarely incorporate other parameters describing chromatographic processes—such as volumetric flow rate, dead volume, dead time, phase ratio, and retention time. This study integrates these parameters into the plate-theory mathematical model to establish a comprehensive model with mobile-phase runtime as the independent variable, thereby laying the chemical foundation for constructing an interactive plate-theory teaching tool.

1.1 Common Models of Plate Theory

If a chromatographic column comprises nn theoretical plates, then the solute concentration in the mobile phase at the column outlet is given by 式（1） [7]. 式（1） is the Poisson function of plate theory; when the number of plates nn is sufficiently large, it closely approximates a Gaussian distribution function.

C=11+KC01n!e−vvnC = \frac{1}{1 + K}C_{0}\frac{1}{n!}e^{- v}v^{n}

In this expression, CC and C0C_{0} denote the solute concentration at the column outlet and the initial concentration, respectively, and KK is the distribution coefficient. 式（2） defines vv [7], representing a physical quantity related to the reduced plate volume, expressed in units of "plate volume" rather than mL.

v=VKvs+vmv = \frac{V}{Kv_{s} + v_{m}}

In this expression, VV is the consumed mobile-phase volume, while vsv_{s} and vmv_{m} denote the volumes of stationary and mobile phases per theoretical plate, respectively.

1.2 Approximate Transformation of the Plate-Theory Model

Because 式（1） involves factorial computation—which entails substantial computational overhead and hinders subsequent simulation—it requires approximation. When n>10n > 10, the factorial may be computed via Stirling’s approximation:

n!≈2πn(ne)nn! \approx \sqrt[{}]{2\pi n}{(\frac{n}{e})}^{n}

Substituting 式（3） into 式（1） yields:

C=11+KC012πn(vn)nen−vC = \frac{1}{1 + K}C_{0}\frac{1}{\sqrt[{}]{2\pi n}}{(\frac{v}{n})}^{n}e^{n - v}

1.3 Introduction of the Time Variable into the Plate-Theory Model

After the above transformations, the plate-theory model is mathematically expressed by the dynamic equation described in 式（4） and the constraint condition in 式（2）. If KK , vsv_{s}, vmv_{m}, nn, and C0C_{0} are all constants, then CC becomes a function of vv, yielding only a CC–vv plot rather than the conventional CC–tt chromatogram; therefore, the time variable must be introduced into the constraint condition 式（2）.

The consumed mobile-phase volume VV equals the product of the mobile-phase volumetric flow rate FF and the runtime tt:

V=F⋅tV = F \cdot t

Similarly, the dead volume VmV_{m} equals the product of the mobile-phase volumetric flow rate FF and the dead time tmt_{m}:

Vm=F⋅tmV_{m} = F \cdot t_{m}

Because the chromatographic column consists of nn theoretical plates, the dead volume equals the product of the mobile-phase volume per plate vmv_{m} and nn:

Vm=n⋅vmV_{m} = n \cdot v_{m}

The phase ratio β\beta satisfies the following relationship:

β=VmVs=nvmnvs=vmvs\beta = \frac{V_{m}}{V_{s}} = \frac{nv_{m}}{nv_{s}} = \frac{v_{m}}{v_{s}}

Substituting Equations (5)–(8) into 式（2） yields:

v=VKvs+vm=Ft(K/β+1)vm=Ft(K/β+1)Vm/n=tn(K/β+1)tmv = \frac{V}{Kv_{s} + v_{m}} = \frac{Ft}{(K/\beta + 1)v_{m}} = \frac{Ft}{(K/\beta + 1)V_{m}/n} = \frac{tn}{(K/\beta + 1)t_{m}}

Thus, the integrated plate-theory model comprises the dynamic equation described in 式（4） and the time-variable-integrated constraint condition in 式（9）. Consequently, when KK , vsv_{s}, vmv_{m}, nn, and C0C_{0} are all constants, CC becomes a function of tt, theoretically enabling generation of a CC–tt plot conforming to the typical shape of a chromatogram.

To ensure smooth implementation of teaching activities and uninterrupted student learning, an open-source, free software platform must be selected. The GeoGebra cloud-based computational platform not only satisfies these criteria but also supports multi-terminal operation (mobile phones, tablets, desktop computers) and features programming syntax closely resembling natural language—making it exceptionally well-suited for classroom use [12, 13]. During the in-class model construction phase, we employed knowledge-transfer pedagogy. We restructured the textbook framework, built a coherent knowledge system, and increased classroom interaction frequency by 23% through knowledge-transfer pedagogy. During the in-class model application phase, we implemented a three-tier progressive module-based instruction.

Module 1: Single-component simulation. For disciplines lacking hands-on chromatography laboratory courses (e.g., environmental science), Case 1 enriches students’ chromatographic learning experience by designing a near-realistic chromatographic experiment and assigning the task of simulating a single-component chromatographic elution profile. Leveraging an open-source, free cloud computing platform and conducting pre-class warm-up activities, students achieved barrier-free operation of the cloud platform when simulating single-component elution profiles. Real-time generation of simulated chromatograms stimulated students’ intrinsic motivation to showcase their learning outcomes; 94% voluntarily shared screenshots of their results.

Module 2: Multi-component simulation. In Case 2, students encountered the most challenging operational issue: how to input numerical values for bivariate functions. By guiding students to observe 式（4） and Equation (9), they recognize that, besides time tt, the parameter KK—which describes solute partitioning between stationary and mobile phases—is another critical variable influencing CC. 4 Conclusion

This study successfully developed a three-tier progressive chromatographic teaching tool featuring dynamic parameter adjustment by integrating the GeoGebra platform with AI technologies. The tool effectively mitigates the abstraction inherent in plate-theory instruction, achieving a 94% classroom task completion rate and a 76% accuracy rate in retention time equation derivation. Practice confirms that the "digital modeling (AI-assisted derivation via 'Doubao')–parameter interaction (multiple adjustable chromatographic parameters)–visual verification (dynamic fitting of chromatographic elution profiles)" paradigm significantly boosts student engagement (85% of students independently reused the tool in experiments) and, owing to its open-source architecture, provides a scalable, replicable exemplar for digital pedagogical reform in analytical chemistry.
